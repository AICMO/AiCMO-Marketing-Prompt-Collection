## Overview
Runs AI-powered competitive war games — simulating likely competitor moves 3-12 months in advance — so your positioning, messaging, battlecards, and sales enablement are ready before the threat materializes. Unlike reactive competitive event response, war gaming is proactive: you model scenarios (competitor fundraises, launches a free tier, acquires an adjacent tool, poaches your champion), stress-test your defenses, identify blind spots, and pre-build the playbooks that will protect pipeline and win rate. Use this quarterly to stress-test your competitive position, before a board review, or when signals suggest a major competitor move is coming.

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: Scenarios feel generic and not specific enough to be useful**
Solution: Feed in more precise competitive intelligence — actual job postings, pricing page screenshots, conference demo transcripts, G2 review themes. The more specific your inputs about what each competitor is actually doing, the more realistic and actionable the Red Team scenarios become. Run the prompt with a specific recent competitor signal as the anchor, not just general competitive context.

**Problem: Sales team ignores pre-built battlecard updates from the war game**
Solution: Flip the format — instead of a document, run a 30-minute war game session live with the top 5 AEs. Have them play the Red Team (competitor reps) attacking deals. The experiential learning from playing the attacker creates far more durable knowledge than reading a battlecard. Record the session and share clips in the #competitive-war-room Slack channel.

**Problem: War game scenarios seem too speculative to justify the investment in pre-building responses**
Solution: Calculate the Expected Value of preparation: (Probability of scenario × ARR at risk × % protected with preparation) - cost to prepare. For a scenario with 40% probability, $500K ARR at risk, and 60% protection improvement, the expected value is $120K protected. A 4-hour prep session costing $1,500 in team time has a 80x ROI. Frame the war game as insurance with calculable premiums and payouts.
