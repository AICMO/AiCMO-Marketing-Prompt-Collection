## Integration Tips

- **Greenhouse / Lever / Workday Recruiting:** Build the Screening Scorecard as a custom "Scorecard" in your ATS — interviewers score directly inside the system so you aggregate results automatically. Map each interview round to a stage gate with minimum score thresholds before advancing.
- **HireVue / Spark Hire (Video Interviews):** Use the Round 2 behavioral questions as video prompts for asynchronous screening — saves 3-4 hours of live interview time per hire and creates a reviewable record for hiring manager calibration.
- **Notion / Confluence (Interview Prep):** Build a templated "Interview Prep Pack" in Notion that auto-populates from your ATS candidate profile: role context, candidate's resume highlights, assigned questions per round, and scoring rubric. Share view-only access with each interviewer 24 hours before their round.
- **Slack:** Create a private channel per open role (#hiring-demand-gen-sr, etc.) where interviewers drop debrief notes within 2 hours of each interview. Use a Slack bot prompt to enforce structure: "Rate the candidate 1-4 overall. What was the strongest signal? What's your biggest concern? Advance or decline?" This creates a written record and prevents the most senior voice from anchoring the room in live debriefs.
- **ChatGPT / Claude (Job Description Drafting):** Paste the Internal Role Brief into Claude with the prompt: "You are a senior B2B SaaS marketing talent acquisition specialist. Write a 180-word external job posting for this role that leads with business impact, names specific AI tool requirements, and avoids all corporate filler language. The posting should attract a senior IC who has already succeeded at this level and is looking for a harder, more impactful challenge." Edit the output to add your specific company voice.
- **LinkedIn Talent Insights:** Before posting, run a LinkedIn Talent Insights report on your target candidate pool. Understand the supply of qualified candidates in your market, their current employers, typical compensation bands, and skill prevalence. Use this data to set realistic timelines and calibrate your must-have criteria vs. nice-to-haves.
- **Loom:** Record a 3-minute "Why this role exists and why I'm excited to hire for it" video from the hiring manager. Embed in the job posting and the take-home assignment instructions. Candidates who watch it are 40% more likely to complete the assignment and accept an offer.

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: The take-home assignment is turning away great candidates who say it's too long.**
Solution: The 90-minute ceiling is your protection — if candidates are spending 4+ hours, the scenario is too complex or the brief is too vague. Audit your take-home against this checklist: (1) Is the scenario specific enough that a candidate knows exactly what to include vs. exclude? (2) Have you explicitly stated it's 90 minutes, and that a focused 90-minute submission beats an exhaustive 4-hour one? (3) Are you asking for strategy + execution + analysis all in one? Split it: ask for strategy only, and test execution judgment in the interview instead. For candidates who still decline to complete take-homes, offer a live 45-minute working session as an alternative — it tests the same dimensions with more time efficiency for them.

**Problem: The AI competency questions are producing inconsistent results — some candidates give great answers, but we can't tell if they're genuine AI users or just well-prepared for AI interview questions.**
Solution: Move the AI competency assessment from interview questions to embedded observation. Add this instruction to the take-home: "Please share one screenshot or screen recording from your AI tool usage while working on this assignment." Real AI users will have no trouble with this; performative AI claimants won't be able to produce authentic evidence. Additionally, ask them to walk you through their AI workflow live in Round 2 — give them 5 minutes to show you their most recent AI-assisted work in their current/most recent role using their actual setup. Live observation cuts through prepared answers.

**Problem: Reference checks are coming back uniformly positive, giving us no useful signal.**
Solution: The problem is the framing. References default to positive unless you make honest feedback feel safe and specifically requested. Reframe your opening: "I'm going to ask you some specific questions — I need you to give me the same honest assessment you'd give a trusted colleague who was considering hiring them, not a formal HR reference." Then ask: "On a team of 10 people at this level, where would you rank them?" Most references will name a number. Any rank below 5 requires follow-up. Also ask: "What is the one thing their next manager should know to manage them well?" — this question almost always surfaces a real development area that wouldn't emerge from "what are their weaknesses?"
