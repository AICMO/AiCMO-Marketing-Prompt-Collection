## Troubleshooting

**Problem: Journalists keep describing us in terms of the old category, ignoring our new framing.**
Solution: Stop pitching the category directly — journalists resist being told what to write. Instead, pitch DATA stories that only make sense through your category lens. When they cover the data, they'll adopt your framing organically. Also: seed the terminology with 5-10 practitioner-influencers whose language journalists quote; human sources adopting your language is more persuasive than your PR pitches.

**Problem: Analysts aren't convinced our category is distinct enough from existing categories.**
Solution: The key failure mode is insufficient "boundary conditions" — you haven't clearly defined what's IN vs. OUT of the category. Create a decision matrix: "A solution IS [Category Name] if it does X, Y, Z. It is NOT [Category Name] if it does A, B, or C." Present this boundary condition framework to analysts with customer examples on both sides of the line. Analysts need to be able to classify vendors unambiguously to create a research category.

**Problem: Competitors are adopting our category language, diluting our ownership.**
Solution: This is actually a leading indicator of success — your category is working. Accelerate to the "definition control" phase: publish the formal category definition on your website with a date stamp, file for trademark on key phrases if applicable, and intensify your original research program so you're the *source* of category data. When competitors use your language, they're implicitly validating your authority. Brief analysts on the "category copycats" dynamic and position it as evidence of category momentum.
