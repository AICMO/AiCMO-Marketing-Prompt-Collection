**DELIVERABLE 6: SALES & CUSTOMER SUCCESS OBJECTION PLAYBOOK**

Generate word-for-word responses to the 8 objections sales and CS teams will hear starting on announcement day. For each, provide: the fear beneath the objection, the response framework, exact language to use, and exact language to avoid.

OBJECTION 1: "We need to pause our renewal conversation until the dust settles."
Fear beneath: "This deal changes what I'm buying and I don't want to commit to something that might change."
Response: Acknowledge the instinct; reframe the timing advantage; offer the retention commitment as a reason to commit now rather than later.
Language to avoid: "I understand your concern but..." / "Nothing is changing..." (both telegraph defensiveness)

OBJECTION 2: "Is our CSM leaving?"
Fear beneath: "The relationship I've built and the institutional knowledge my CSM has is the real product I'm buying."
Response: Specific answer with a name and a commitment, not a "we're committed to continuity" deflection.

OBJECTION 3: "Are you going to raise our prices?"
Fear beneath: "Acquisitions always lead to pricing power moves. I need to lock in my rate now."
Response: The specific pricing commitment the company has made, the duration, and what triggers a renegotiation if any.

OBJECTION 4: "Will [ACQUIRED COMPANY PRODUCT] be sunsetted and forced onto [ACQUIRER PRODUCT]?"
Fear beneath: "I chose this product for a reason and I don't want to be forced into a migration."
Response: Product roadmap commitment with specific dates and the "what triggers any migration" honest answer.

OBJECTION 5: "I've heard [ACQUIRER NAME] has a reputation for [negative thing] — is that going to affect us?"
Fear beneath: "I've done my research and I'm worried about what I found."
Response: Acknowledge the reputation issue directly (avoidance is worse); address the specific claim; provide evidence of different behavior.

OBJECTION 6: "We were evaluating [COMPETITOR] — does this change anything about your capabilities?"
Fear beneath: "This acquisition may have actually made my status quo easier to justify."
Response: How the acquisition strengthens the competitive position specifically against the competitor they named.

OBJECTION 7: "What happens to our data if this integration goes wrong?"
Fear beneath: "My data is my most valuable asset and M&A is a security and compliance risk."
Response: Data portability commitments, security review timeline, and the contractual protections that survive the acquisition.

OBJECTION 8: "Can I talk to someone from [ACQUIRED COMPANY]'s executive team directly?"
Fear beneath: "I trust my existing vendor — I don't trust the acquirer yet."
Response: Immediate connection to the acquired company's CEO or CPO; no deflection to the acquiring company's executives until trust is established.

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: The deal leaks to press or social media before the planned announcement time**
Solution: Activate the emergency acceleration protocol immediately — do not wait for your planned announcement time. Step 1: notify both CEOs that the leak has been confirmed. Step 2: send the employee communications within 15 minutes of confirming the leak — employees must not read about their own acquisition on Twitter. Step 3: lift the press release embargo immediately and notify any journalists in briefing that the embargo is lifted due to leak. Step 4: post both CEOs' LinkedIn statements within 30 minutes. Attempting to suppress or deny a confirmed leak is impossible and destroys credibility; accelerated transparency is the only viable response.

**Problem: Key acquired company employees (especially engineers or revenue-critical roles) announce departures in the first 72 hours**
Solution: Treat this as a customer-facing event, not just an HR event — customer confidence is directly tied to the perception that key product people are staying. Step 1: CEO personally calls the departing employee to understand the decision; do not outsource this. Step 2: prepare a factual, human response for any press inquiries that mentions the departure — "We can confirm [Name] has made the personal decision to pursue other opportunities; we respect that and wish them well" — never say "we're not commenting." Step 3: brief the top 20 acquired company customers before they see it on LinkedIn, with a clear message about product continuity regardless of any individual departure.

**Problem: An acquired company customer initiates a cancellation conversation citing the acquisition as the reason**
Solution: Route immediately to acquired company CEO or CPO — not to the acquirer's CSM team. The customer's trust relationship is with the acquired company's leadership, and having an acquirer representative "handle" the churn conversation will accelerate the departure. The CEO or CPO call has one objective: understand the specific fear, make the specific commitment that addresses it, and offer a contractual protection (pricing lock extension, 60-day exit clause if specific conditions are not met) that converts the cancellation conversation into a retention conversation. Customers who threaten to leave and are handled personally at the executive level have a higher retention rate than customers who never raised a concern.
