## Troubleshooting

**Problem: Attribution data shows email influenced many deals but revenue credit looks inflated.**
Solution: Switch from "any-touch" to "time-decay" or "linear multi-touch" attribution and rerun. Any-touch overcredits email because it is the highest-frequency touchpoint in most B2B nurture programs. Time-decay model (weights touchpoints closer to deal close more heavily) typically produces more defensible email attribution numbers for executive presentations.

**Problem: Open rate data is unreliable due to Apple Mail Privacy Protection (MPP).**
Solution: Deprioritize open rate as the primary KPI and substitute CTOR (click-to-open rate using actual clicks as numerator and estimated true opens as denominator) + click rate as your engagement benchmarks. MPP inflates raw open rates by 20–40% for B2B audiences with high Apple Mail usage (typically 35–55% of opens). Flag this in the executive scorecard with a methodology note. Use "confirmed engagements" (clicks + replies + conversions) as your true engagement count.

**Problem: Cohort analysis shows re-engaged contacts have much lower CTOR than recently acquired contacts.**
Solution: This is normal and expected — re-engaged contacts typically perform 40–60% below freshly acquired contacts for the first 3–6 months. Segment them separately in your scorecard and track velocity of improvement rather than absolute performance. Keep them on a lower-frequency, higher-value content track until engagement recovers. If CTOR doesn't improve after 3 sends in 90 days, sunset permanently.
