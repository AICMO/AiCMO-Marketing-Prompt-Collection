5. LEARNING LOOP DESIGN — CAMPAIGN-TO-KNOWLEDGE FLYWHEEL

Design the system that converts every campaign result into a structured future-performance asset:

**THE FLYWHEEL MECHANICS:**

Phase 1 — HYPOTHESIS CAPTURE (pre-launch)
Every campaign begins with a structured hypothesis in testable format:
"We believe that [specific audience segment] will respond to [specific creative/offer/channel] because [specific reason based on prior knowledge], resulting in [specific measurable outcome]. We'll validate this at [sample size] with [statistical threshold]."
→ This forces explicit assumption documentation, enabling precise future learning.

Phase 2 — SIGNAL MONITORING (in-flight)
Automated weekly performance snapshot logged to campaign record:
- Actual vs. predicted performance on primary metric (% variance)
- Emerging audience behavior signals (unexpected segments engaging, unexpected content consumption patterns)
- External market events that may be influencing performance
→ Creates a time-stamped performance narrative, not just a final result.

Phase 3 — RESULT SYNTHESIS (post-campaign, within 72 hours of close)
Structured retrospective generates 3 types of knowledge entries:
- VALIDATED LEARNING: "We now know with high confidence that [X] because [evidence]. This changes how we'll approach [future scenario]."
- INVALIDATED ASSUMPTION: "We assumed [X] but learned [Y]. Previous campaigns built on this assumption should be reviewed."
- OPEN QUESTION: "This campaign raised the question of whether [X]. Design of next experiment to answer it."
→ Each entry is immediately routed to relevant knowledge taxonomy tier.

Phase 4 — KNOWLEDGE ACTIVATION (immediate distribution)
New learnings auto-formatted and distributed to:
- Relevant AI context documents (auto-appended to Campaign Learning Feed)
- Slack #marketing-intelligence channel (human-readable digest format)
- AI tools system prompts (for teams using AI with custom instructions)
- Related campaign records (cross-referenced for pattern building)
→ Knowledge is live and usable within 48 hours of campaign close.

Phase 5 — PATTERN RECOGNITION (quarterly synthesis)
Quarterly automated prompt to knowledge base: "Review all campaign records from the past 90 days and identify: (1) Emerging patterns in what's working across channels, (2) Consistent failure modes appearing in multiple campaigns, (3) Audience behavior shifts vs. prior quarter, (4) Hypothesis accuracy rate — are we getting better at predicting outcomes?"
→ Output: Quarterly Learning Synthesis Report distributed to full marketing team.

**FLYWHEEL ACCELERATION METRICS:**
- Hypothesis accuracy rate (target: improve from baseline by 10pp per quarter)
- Campaign performance variance from prediction (target: <25% variance at steady state)
- Time from campaign close to knowledge activation (target: <48 hours)
- Knowledge base query-to-decision rate (% of queries that lead to documented decision changes — target: >40%)

6. KNOWLEDGE DECAY MANAGEMENT SYSTEM

Design the system that prevents stale knowledge from contaminating future decisions and AI outputs:

**DECAY CLASSIFICATION:**

Tier 1 — FAST DECAY (review every 90 days):
- Competitive intelligence entries
- Paid channel performance benchmarks
- Seasonal engagement patterns
- AI tool prompt effectiveness ratings
Decay trigger: Market conditions change faster than 90-day review cycle
Auto-alert: Notion database automation flags entries approaching review date 2 weeks in advance

Tier 2 — MEDIUM DECAY (review every 6 months):
- ICP segment profiles
- Campaign performance patterns by channel
- Vendor/agency scorecards
- Messaging effectiveness data
Decay trigger: Significant product launch, market category shift, or major competitive event
Auto-alert: Bi-annual review scheduled in team calendar automatically

Tier 3 — SLOW DECAY (review annually):
- Brand voice calibration documents
- Audience psychographic profiles
- Win/loss pattern database (pattern level, not individual records)
- Category narrative and positioning foundation
Decay trigger: Significant company pivot, new market entry, or rebranding initiative
Auto-alert: Annual knowledge base audit scheduled in Q4

**STALENESS DETECTION AUTOMATION:**
Zapier/Make workflow: Every 30 days, scan Notion knowledge base for entries where:
- Freshness date > 90 days AND Tier 1 classification → Flag as URGENT REVIEW
- Freshness date > 180 days AND Tier 2 classification → Flag as SCHEDULED REVIEW
- Freshness date > 365 days AND Tier 3 classification → Flag as ANNUAL REVIEW
Auto-create review tasks in Asana/ClickUp with entry owner assigned

**KNOWLEDGE POISONING PREVENTION:**
When AI tools generate outputs that team members identify as "based on outdated knowledge," mandatory process:
1. Log the specific knowledge entry that caused the bad output
2. Flag entry as STALE — immediately quarantined from AI context documents
3. Assign knowledge refresh task to owner (3-day SLA)
4. Post-refresh: Document what changed and why in version history
5. Notify all AI tool users of context document update

**GRACEFUL KNOWLEDGE RETIREMENT:**
Entries that can't be refreshed (e.g., team member who owned the knowledge has left) are moved to ARCHIVED status with a note: "Confidence LOW — original context unavailable. Use as directional only. Do not use in AI tool context documents until re-validated."

OUTPUT FORMAT:
Deliver all 6 sections as an implementation-ready playbook. For each section, include:
- The strategic framework (what and why)
- The exact implementation steps (how, who, which tools, how long)
- A "week one quickstart" — the minimum viable version to activate in the first 5 business days
- The leading indicator that tells you the system is working before you see revenue impact

**Input Example:**

Company: Stackline — B2B SaaS retail intelligence platform for mid-market and enterprise consumer brands. Competes with Jungle Scout, Helium 10 (for SMB), and custom BI solutions (for enterprise). $14M ARR, Series A. Marketing team of 7, average tenure 11 months. Core pain: "We've rebuilt our demand gen playbook three times in 18 months because key people left. Our new demand gen manager is making the same LinkedIn Ads mistakes we solved in 2023."

**Output Example (Campaign Knowledge Record — Excerpted):**

---
**CAMPAIGN RECORD: LinkedIn Sponsored Content — Enterprise Brand Managers — Q2 2023**
*Captured by: Maya Rodriguez, Demand Gen Manager | Validated by: CMO | Confidence: HIGH*
*Freshness date: Reviewed Q1 2025 — patterns confirmed still valid*

**HYPOTHESIS:**
We believed that Enterprise Brand Managers (at consumer brands $100M+ revenue) would respond to LinkedIn single-image ads featuring "retail analytics ROI" messaging because of their Q2 planning cycle pressure, resulting in a 2.5% CTR and $280 CPL. Validated at n=4,200 impressions, 95% confidence.

**WHAT ACTUALLY HAPPENED:**
CTR: 1.1% (underperformed by 56%). CPL: $610 (underperformed by 118%).

**ROOT CAUSE ANALYSIS:**
Audience segment was too broad — "Brand Managers" at large consumer brands includes junior roles without budget authority. Retargeted to "Senior Brand Director" and "VP Brand" titles only.

**WHAT WE DID NEXT:**
Narrowed targeting to Seniority: Director+ AND Job Function: Marketing AND Company Size: 1,000+. Shifted creative from ROI framing to "retail intelligence that makes you the smartest person in your exec team's meeting" — executive status framing tested against ROI framing.

## Troubleshooting
**Problem: Knowledge capture triggers are ignored — team members skip the forms and the knowledge base goes empty.**
Solution: Remove optionality from the highest-leverage triggers. The campaign close-out retrospective (Trigger 4) must gate budget release or be required for a campaign to be marked complete in the project management tool. Without a consequence, forms are perpetually "I'll do it later." Start with making two triggers mandatory: campaign close-out and team member offboarding. These two alone create 70% of institutional knowledge value. Make all others voluntary but celebrated publicly when completed.

**Problem: AI tools don't noticeably improve even after context documents are added.**
Solution: Context document quality is almost always the issue, not context quantity. A 3,000-word brand voice document that doesn't include specific examples of on-brand vs. off-brand copy will produce minimal improvement. Audit every context document against this test: "Could someone who has never heard of our company produce something that sounds exactly like us using only this document?" If no, rewrite with more specific examples, more vocabulary calibration, and more explicit anti-patterns (what we never sound like). Keep context documents under 800 words per document — AI tools perform better with focused, high-density context than with exhaustive encyclopedias.

**Problem: The knowledge base becomes politically sensitive — team members resist capturing failures or "what we'd do differently" honestly.**
Solution: Separate knowledge capture from performance management. Make it explicit that knowledge records are not used in performance reviews and are accessible only to the marketing team (not HR, not executives outside marketing). Reframe the system culturally: capturing a failure clearly and analytically is evidence of high performance, not of failure. Leaders must model this — the CMO should be the first person to contribute a detailed "what I'd do differently" entry. Anonymize entries where needed in the first 90 days to build psychological safety, then transition to named entries once the culture shifts.
