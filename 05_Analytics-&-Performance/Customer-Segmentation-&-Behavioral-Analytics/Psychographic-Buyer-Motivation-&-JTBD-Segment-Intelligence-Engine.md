## Overview
Synthesizes customer interviews, NPS responses, support tickets, win/loss data, and usage patterns to uncover distinct Jobs-to-be-Done segments — the specific functional, social, and emotional outcomes customers are "hiring" your product to achieve. Use this when demographic segmentation isn't explaining revenue concentration, when campaigns aren't converting despite good targeting, or when you need to discover which customer archetype drives the highest LTV and fewest churn events.

1. **Switch Interview Framework (Bob Moesta)** — Reconstruct the "struggling moment" for each JTBD segment using the four forces: push (away from old solution), pull (toward your solution), anxiety (about switching), and habit (allegiance to status quo). 2. **Outcome-Driven Innovation (Tony Ulwick)** — For each job, identify the outcome statement (job executor + metric + object + context). Score each outcome on importance (how critical) × satisfaction gap (how poorly current solutions perform). High-importance, low-satisfaction = unmet JTBD = your highest-potential segment.

3. **Jobs Atlas (Klement)** — Map jobs hierarchically: the big job (core desired progress), supporting jobs (steps needed to accomplish the big job), emotional jobs (how they want to feel), and social jobs (how they want to be perceived by others). **Segment Story — The Stockout Firefighter:**
These buyers just got burned — or are terrified of getting burned again. They're typically VP of Merchandising or Inventory Planning Managers at retail brands doing $50M-$500M revenue who experienced at least one major revenue-losing stockout in the past 12 months. They're not searching for a "data analytics platform" — they're searching for stockout prevention. They'll pay premium pricing if you can prove you prevent the specific event that woke them up at 2am. Marketing to this segment means meeting them at the moment of pain: search terms like "prevent holiday stockouts," industry forums post-stockout events, and LinkedIn content about inventory risk. Retention is high because switching carries emotional risk — once they've solved the nightmare, they don't want to risk experiencing it again.

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: All customers seem to have the same job — my segments are too similar to differentiate.**
Fix: You're likely describing the job at too high a level. Dig one layer deeper using the "why" ladder: if the job is "improve inventory management," ask "why does that matter?" until you hit an emotional or social outcome. "Improve inventory management" → "prevent stockouts" → "avoid revenue loss" → "keep my job and look competent to the board." Different customers stop at different rungs — that's where the real segment differentiation lives. Also check: are churned and retained customers saying the same thing? If so, look at usage data for behavioral divergence, not self-reported jobs.

**Problem: I don't have enough qualitative data — I only have product usage metrics and CRM fields.**
Fix: You can still build provisional JTBD segments using behavioral proxy signals. Map your highest-retention customers by the features they use most in their first 30 days — different feature clusters often correlate with different jobs. Then add firmographic patterns (company type, growth rate, tech stack) to identify which customer types cluster into each behavioral group. Run a 5-customer "switch interview" sprint to validate your hypotheses with real stories before launching full-segment campaigns.

**Problem: The sales team says JTBD segments don't match how prospects talk in calls.**
Fix: This is a calibration opportunity, not a failure. Run a 30-minute JTBD session with 3-5 AEs using real recent call recordings. Ask reps to categorize each deal into the JTBD segments and note where prospects' language doesn't match. Update struggling moment descriptions and segment language to reflect how buyers actually describe their situation. The goal is JTBD segments that AEs immediately recognize from their pipeline — not academic frameworks that don't survive contact with a sales call.
