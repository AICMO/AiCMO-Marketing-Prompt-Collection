## Success Metrics

A high-quality Amazon Ads performance audit output should deliver:

- **TACOS trajectory identification**: Clearly establishes whether total account economics are improving or deteriorating — a declining TACOS alongside improving or stable ACOS confirms flywheel momentum
- **Wasted spend recovery**: Identifies search term waste representing at least 5–15% of total ad spend that can be recovered through negative keyword additions within one week
- **NTB economics clarity**: Calculates the true 12-month NTB customer acquisition cost, enabling comparison to direct channel CAC (often Amazon NTB CAC is 30–50% lower than paid social equivalents for physical products)

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: ACOS improving but total revenue is flat or declining (TACOS rising)**
This is the most dangerous Amazon Ads misread: optimizing ACOS in isolation while organic rank collapses. It typically happens when you reduce spend on broad/auto campaigns to improve ACOS efficiency — these campaigns also drive the sales velocity that sustains organic rank. The algorithm interprets sales deceleration as reduced demand and lowers your organic position, requiring more ad spend to compensate. Fix: Never reduce ad spend without first checking BSR trend for the affected ASINs. Set a minimum acceptable organic rank threshold for each top ASIN (e.g., "never let 'diamond cutting blade' organic rank fall below #15") and treat ad spend required to maintain that threshold as non-negotiable. Use Sponsored Products auto campaigns as rank insurance, not just discovery vehicles — they generate impressions and sales velocity across a wide keyword set even when individual term economics look marginal.

**Problem: High NTB% on Sponsored Display remarketing campaigns (appears counterintuitive)**
Sponsored Display "views remarketing" audiences include users who viewed your product page in the last 30 days — but many of these viewers are genuinely new customers who haven't purchased yet. If NTB% on SD is above 40%, you're not retargeting buyers; you're running consideration-stage ads to warm audiences who discovered you organically or via other campaigns. This is actually valuable but requires a different success metric than retargeting. Fix: Separate SD campaigns into three distinct buckets with separate ACOS targets: (1) Purchase Remarketing — existing customer cross-sell/upsell, target ACOS 35–50% (LTV math justifies it), (2) Views Remarketing — warm non-buyers, target ACOS at your NTB acquisition target, (3) Product Targeting — appear on competitor PDPs, target ACOS 25–35% as conquest economics. Mixing these in one campaign produces misleading blended ACOS and prevents proper budget allocation.

**Problem: Sponsored Brands brand defense campaign showing low ACOS (14%) but minimal incremental value**
Very low ACOS on brand defense campaigns often reflects brand-name searches from existing customers who would have found and purchased organically without the ad. The question is not "what is the ACOS?" but "what is the incremental revenue from showing the ad vs. not showing it?" Fix: Run a holdout test — pause brand defense Sponsored Brands for 2 weeks (not Sponsored Products, which also defends against competitors on PDPs), monitor whether organic brand search revenue changes materially. For most established brands, brand defense SB incremental lift is 3–8% of attributed revenue (competitors fill the brand search vacuum only partially). If your brand defense campaign budget is above $2,000/month and you haven't run an incrementality test in 12 months, you are likely over-investing in non-incremental brand traffic.
