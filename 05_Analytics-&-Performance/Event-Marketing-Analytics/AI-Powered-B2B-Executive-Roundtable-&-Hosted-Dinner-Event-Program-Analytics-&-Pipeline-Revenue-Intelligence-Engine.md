## Troubleshooting

**Problem: Attribution double-counting — the same deal is being counted as both "sourced" and "influenced."**
Solution: Define a clear attribution hierarchy rule before running the analysis: if an opportunity existed before the event date, it is always "influenced" regardless of whether it advanced significantly after the event. Only opportunities created with no prior stage for the account can be tagged "sourced." Apply this rule consistently in Salesforce by using the Opportunity Created Date vs. Event Date as the decision point.

**Problem: Low show rate (under 50%) — hard to generate meaningful analytics from a thin data set.**
Solution: Before analyzing ROI, run a no-show root cause analysis: (1) Was the event promoted to confirmed attendees with reminders 48h and 24h before? (2) Were invitees truly ICP-fit, or were they added to fill seats? (3) Was the topic relevant and specific enough to create felt urgency? A low show rate invalidates the event ROI numbers — flag this clearly in the report and prioritize show-rate improvement over pipeline attribution in the recommendations. For future events: confirm guests with personal AE calls 72h before; limit guest list to only accounts where an AE or SDR has an active relationship.

**Problem: No post-event engagement — most attendees went dark after the event.**
Solution: Diagnose follow-up lag and personalization quality first. If follow-up happened within 24 hours and was highly personalized, the issue is likely guest list quality (attendees who weren't truly evaluating). If follow-up was delayed (>72 hours) or generic ("Great to meet you, let's connect"), the event generated intent but the follow-up failed to capitalize on it. For immediately actionable recovery: have the hosting executive make personal calls to the top 5 most-valuable attendees with zero post-event engagement, referencing a specific moment from the dinner — executive-to-executive outreach typically achieves 3–4× higher response rate than AE outreach.
