### Analysis Framework

**Step 1 — Revenue Attribution Model**
Build a multi-category revenue waterfall:
- Category A: New logo pipeline directly sourced (prospect attended → opportunity created)
- Category B: Pipeline acceleration (existing opp, stage moved faster post-event)
- Category C: Closed-won deals with conference as last marketing touch or key acceleration point
- Category D: Expansion ARR lift (attendee vs. non-attendee cohort delta)
- Category E: Churn prevention value (attendee retention rate × ARR at risk × retention lift vs. non-attendee cohort)
- Category F: NPS-to-ARR value (NPS point improvement × historical NPS/retention correlation × ARR at risk)
- Category G: Sponsor and partner revenue
- Category H: Brand/awareness (apply a conservative 0.5× multiplier on PR/analyst coverage and social reach)

Apply time-decay weighting: 100% for outcomes within 90 days, 70% for 91–180 days, 40% for 181–365 days.

**Step 2 — ROI Calculation**
- Conservative ROI: Categories A + C + G only (fully attributable, short cycle)
- Base ROI: All categories A through G at face value
- Optimistic ROI: All categories A through H with full attribution
- Return per $1 invested: Total attributed revenue ÷ total cost
- Breakeven analysis: What ARR attribution is required to justify this conference at 1:1, 3:1, and 5:1 ROI?

**Step 3 — Churn Prevention Quantification (CSAT/NPS to ARR model)**
- Calculate attendee churn rate vs. non-attendee churn rate (matched cohort by ARR tier, industry, contract duration)
- Retention lift = non-attendee churn % minus attendee churn %
- Retention lift value = ARR at risk in attendee cohort × retention lift %
- NPS lift value = (post-event NPS − pre-event NPS) × (estimated revenue per NPS point based on historical cohort data or industry proxy: $X ARR per 1-point NPS increase at your scale)

**Step 4 — Cost Efficiency Benchmarking**
Calculate and benchmark each metric against B2B SaaS conference benchmarks:
| Metric | Your Result | Good | Great | Top Quartile |
|--------|-------------|------|-------|--------------|
| Cost per attendee | $X | <$1,500 | <$1,000 | <$700 |
| Cost per customer attendee | $X | <$2,000 | <$1,200 | <$800 |
| Cost per prospect MQL | $X | <$500 | <$300 | <$150 |
| Cost per new opportunity | $X | <$8,000 | <$5,000 | <$2,500 |
| Cost per closed-won | $X | <$40,000 | <$25,000 | <$12,000 |
| Attendee-to-opportunity rate | X% | >8% | >15% | >25% |
| Sponsor revenue as % of cost | X% | >20% | >40% | >60% |
| Customer attendance rate (% of base) | X% | >10% | >20% | >35% |

**Step 5 — Year-Over-Year Trend Analysis**
If historical data is available:
- ROI trend: improving or deteriorating?
- Attendee growth rate vs. cost growth rate
- Pipeline yield per attendee: improving or declining?
- NPS lift per edition: is the conference getting better at customer impact?

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: "I can't isolate conference attribution — marketing touches every account across multiple channels simultaneously."**
*Fix:* Use a matched cohort approach instead of single-touch attribution. Compare 12-month outcomes for conference attendees vs. a propensity-matched group of non-attendees (same ARR tier, industry, tenure, health score). The delta between cohorts = conference's incremental contribution. This is the same methodology used in marketing mix modeling and is CFO-defensible.

**Problem: "Our churn data shows almost no difference between attendee and non-attendee cohorts."**
*Fix:* Check for selection bias — if only your healthiest customers are self-selecting to attend, you're measuring the wrong thing. The real insight is whether conference attendance *causes* lower churn, which requires measuring at-risk account attendance rates specifically. Segment your attendee data by health score tier: do red/yellow account attendees show materially different retention than matched non-attendees?

**Problem: "The CFO is skeptical of the NPS-to-ARR conversion math."**
*Fix:* Anchor to your own historical data first: pull 3 years of company NPS data and overlay with annual GRR. Calculate the Pearson correlation and use that coefficient as your NPS-to-ARR multiplier. If internal data is thin, use Bain's published research (a 12-point NPS improvement correlates with ~6% revenue growth rate improvement in B2B SaaS) as a conservative proxy, and flag it clearly as an external benchmark.
