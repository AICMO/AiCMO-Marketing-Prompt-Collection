## ANALYSIS FRAMEWORK

### 1. CUSTOMER-AS-CHANNEL (CaC) ROI MODEL

Compare each co-marketing channel to your paid acquisition alternatives using a unified Cost Per Pipeline Dollar metric:

For each activity type:

**Advocate Time Cost** = (Advocate hours per activity × assumed advocate hourly value [$200/hr for VP/Director level]) × Number of activities
**Marketing Support Cost** = Direct costs (events, content production, tool costs)
**Total Investment** = Advocate Time Cost + Marketing Support Cost
**Pipeline Attributed** = Sourced ARR + (Influenced ARR × 0.3 influence weighting factor)
**Cost Per Pipeline Dollar** = Total Investment / Pipeline Attributed

Compare against your paid channel benchmarks:
- Google Ads Cost Per Pipeline Dollar: [$X — pull from your paid media data]
- LinkedIn Ads Cost Per Pipeline Dollar: [$X]
- Content Syndication Cost Per Pipeline Dollar: [$X]
- Events (traditional sponsorships) Cost Per Pipeline Dollar: [$X]

Target: Co-marketing activities should achieve Cost Per Pipeline Dollar ≤ 50% of your most efficient paid channel.

### 2. CO-MARKETING ACTIVITY PORTFOLIO MATRIX

Plot each activity on a 2×2 matrix:
- X-axis: Pipeline impact per activity (low → high)
- Y-axis: Advocate time investment per activity (low → high)

**Quadrant 1 — SCALE (High impact, Low advocate effort):**
Activities that generate strong pipeline with minimal advocate time. These are your highest-priority investments. Typical winners: co-sell introductions, social amplification, targeted webinar appearances.

**Quadrant 2 — OPTIMIZE (High impact, High advocate effort):**
Activities generating strong pipeline but consuming significant advocate time. These are worth doing but need process efficiency. Typical candidates: conference speaking (high travel burden), research co-authoring (high review cycles). Action: Streamline prep, create reusable templates, reduce advocate time investment per activity.

**Quadrant 3 — TEST (Low impact, Low advocate effort):**
Activities with unclear ROI but low advocate investment risk. Experiment with these but cap investment. Typical candidates: social amplification (if ICP match is low) or blog contributions.

**Quadrant 4 — SUNSET (Low impact, High advocate effort):**
Activities consuming advocate goodwill without measurable pipeline return. Eliminate or fundamentally redesign. Being honest about Quadrant 4 activities is how you maintain long-term advocate sustainability.

### 3. DARK SOCIAL PIPELINE MULTIPLIER

Many co-marketing activities (especially conferences and social amplification) generate pipeline through channels that don't appear in CRM source attribution. Apply a Dark Social Multiplier to your direct attribution numbers:

**Dark Social Multiplier by Activity Type:**
- Conference presentations: 2.5–4.0x (each visible pipeline dollar likely represents 2.5–4x more influenced pipeline in dark social)
- Social amplification: 1.8–2.5x (tracked clicks undercount conversations started offline)
- Customer-hosted events: 1.2–1.5x (attendees discuss with colleagues who weren't present)
- Co-authored research: 1.5–2.0x (forwarded, discussed in Slack/Teams without tracking)
- Joint webinars: 1.3–1.6x (replays shared without tracking links)
- Co-sell introductions: 1.0–1.2x (usually directly trackable in CRM)

**Total Co-Marketing Revenue Estimate** = Tracked Pipeline × Weighted Average Dark Social Multiplier

### 4. ADVOCATE SUSTAINABILITY INDEX (ASI)

Measure whether your co-marketing program is building or depleting advocate relationships:

For each active co-marketing advocate:
- Activities completed this quarter: [N]
- Hours invested: [N]
- Perceived value received (survey 1–10): [X]
- Advocacy intention next quarter (will they do more / same / less): [Direction]

**ASI Score = (% of advocates increasing engagement) − (% of advocates reducing engagement)**

Healthy program: ASI > +15 (more advocates ramping up than burning out)
Warning zone: ASI between −5 and +15 (churn and growth roughly balanced)
At-risk program: ASI < −5 (program is extracting more than it's returning to advocates)

Root causes of low ASI:
- Asking advocates for activities before they've seen clear personal benefit (recognition, network value, skills exposure)
- No graduation from easy activities (social sharing) to more demanding ones (research co-authorship) — forcing big asks too early
- Ignoring advocate feedback on what activities they enjoy vs. what feels like work
- Failing to publicly recognize advocate contributions (anonymized recognition is a low-ROI investment; named, specific recognition drives sustained engagement)

### 5. CO-MARKETING CALENDAR OPTIMIZATION

Build next quarter's co-marketing calendar by:

1. **Rank activities by portfolio matrix quadrant** (Quadrant 1 first, Quadrant 4 eliminated)
2. **Match advocate capacity to activity demand** (each advocate has a quarterly "effort budget" — don't exceed it)
3. **Sequence activities by buyer journey stage:**
   - Awareness: Conference speaking, social amplification → drives dark social pipeline building
   - Consideration: Co-authored research, joint webinars → converts aware prospects into pipeline
   - Decision: Customer-hosted events, co-sell introductions → accelerates late-stage deals
4. **Concentrate co-marketing around your highest-priority ICP verticals/segments** (match advocate industry to target prospect industry)

## Troubleshooting

**Issue: Can't prove pipeline attribution for conferences — no tracking mechanism for dark social pipeline that comes from customer presentations**
Solution: Implement a three-layer dark social tracking system. Layer 1: Add a required "How did you hear about us?" field to all demo request forms with options that include specific conference names. Layer 2: Train your AEs to ask in every discovery call "What prompted you to reach out now? Have you seen anyone speaking about [your category] recently?" and log responses in CRM. Layer 3: After each conference, use LinkedIn to identify ICP prospects who follow or connected with your advocate speaker — these are high-intent signals. Combining these three methods typically captures 35–55% of conference-influenced pipeline that would otherwise appear as "direct" or "unknown" in attribution.

**Issue: Advocates are enthusiastic initially but co-marketing activity rates drop sharply after 2 quarters**
Solution: The most common cause is poor activity-to-benefit ratio — advocates invest time but don't see personal career or business value returned. Redesign your co-marketing program to lead with advocate benefits before asking for contribution. Map specific value delivery to each activity: speaking at a conference elevates their personal brand (share speaker metrics and LinkedIn follower growth with them); co-authoring a report positions them as an industry expert (LinkedIn article published under their name, not just a quote); hosting an executive event raises their internal profile with their own leadership. Explicitly track and report "Advocate Career Value Delivered" alongside "Pipeline Generated" — advocates who feel they're growing their own brand are 3–5x more likely to sustain co-marketing engagement beyond 4 quarters.

**Issue: Co-marketing ROI reports show wildly inconsistent results quarter-over-quarter, making it hard to justify program investment**
Solution: Inconsistency usually indicates that co-marketing pipeline is being attributed differently each quarter — sometimes sourced, sometimes influenced, sometimes not tracked at all. Standardize a Co-Marketing Attribution Policy before running this analysis again: (1) Define sourced vs. influenced clearly and apply it consistently, (2) Agree on a dark social multiplier methodology and use it every quarter, (3) Set a minimum data collection standard — if an activity doesn't have CRM touchpoints tracked, it counts as $0 influenced (not an estimate), forcing better tracking discipline. Most programs find that consistent measurement, even if it initially understates ROI, produces more compelling investment justifications over 4+ quarters because the trend line becomes legible.
