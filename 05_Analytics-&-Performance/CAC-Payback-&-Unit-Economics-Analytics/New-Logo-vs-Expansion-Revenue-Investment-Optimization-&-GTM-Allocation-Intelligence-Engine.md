# New Logo vs. Expansion Revenue Investment Optimization & GTM Allocation Intelligence Engine - Determine Exactly How Much to Spend on Acquiring New Customers vs. Expanding Existing Ones

**Difficulty:** Advanced | **Time:** 25-40 min | **Tags:** b2b, saas, unit-economics, expansion-revenue, nrr, cac, gtm-strategy, revenue-operations, land-and-expand

## Overview
Analyzes the comparative unit economics of new logo acquisition vs. existing customer expansion to determine the optimal GTM investment split. Use this when you're deciding how to allocate budget between sales/marketing (new logo) and customer success/upsell programs (expansion), when NRR is becoming a larger share of growth, or when the board asks why you're still spending so much to acquire new customers when existing customers could generate the same ARR at lower cost.

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: "We can't separate CS headcount cost between retention-focused vs. expansion-focused activities — all CSMs do both."**
Solution: Use a time-allocation estimate rather than precise data. Ask your CS team leads: "What % of your team's time goes to proactive expansion vs. reactive retention/support?" A common split is 40% expansion / 60% retention for high-performing CS teams in growth-stage SaaS. Apply this ratio to total CS headcount cost to allocate costs to each motion. Separately, benchmark: if your expansion ARR is < 15% of total ARR and CS headcount is > 8% of total headcount, it's a signal your CS team is retention-heavy. Use this analysis to set a target time allocation for the next quarter and measure improvement in expansion CAC over 6 months.

**Problem: "Our expansion CAC looks amazing on paper, but most expansion ARR comes from one or two enterprise accounts — it's not repeatable."**
Solution: This is the "expansion concentration risk" problem. Recalculate expansion CAC after removing the top 2–3 outlier accounts. If expansion CAC doubles or triples on the remaining portfolio, your expansion motion is not yet systematized — it's relationship-driven and non-scalable. In this case, before rebalancing budget toward expansion, invest first in operationalizing expansion: build a defined playbook, automate trigger identification, and run the expansion motion with at least 20 accounts before measuring CAC. Set a milestone: "We will not shift more than 5% of new logo budget to expansion until expansion close rate exceeds 30% across a minimum of 15 deals."

**Problem: "The board is pushing for NRR improvement but also demanding new logo growth — how do we make the case for rebalancing?"**
Solution: Use the "NRR Compounding vs. New Logo Growth" analysis from Section 6. Build a 3-year model showing two scenarios: (A) Current split — NRR stays at current level, all growth comes from new logos; (B) Rebalanced split — modest reduction in new logo acquisition speed, but NRR improves 3–5 points. In most cases, by Year 2, Scenario B generates equal or greater total ARR because the compounding effect of improved NRR exceeds the short-term new logo ARR loss. The key board message: "Every point of NRR improvement generates $[ARR × 0.01] of additional ARR annually — permanently. That is a better return on our next marginal dollar than buying the next new logo at $[CAC]." Quantify this specifically for your company's numbers to make it undeniable.
