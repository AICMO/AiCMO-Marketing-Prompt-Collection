## Overview
Runs a full unit economics analysis broken down by ICP segment (company size, vertical, geography, buyer persona) to reveal which customer segments generate the highest LTV/CAC ratios, fastest payback periods, and strongest net revenue retention — then produces a prioritized marketing investment reallocation plan. Use this when your marketing budget produces uneven returns across segments and you need rigorous data to decide where to double down, where to pull back, and how to defend those decisions to your CFO and board.

### PART 4: UNIT ECONOMICS CALCULATIONS

Using the data above, calculate the following for EACH segment. Show your work. Flag data gaps and state assumptions explicitly.

**Core metrics:**

1. **Marketing CAC** = (Marketing spend allocated to segment) / (New logos from segment)
2. **Blended CAC** = (Marketing spend + Sales compensation costs attributed to segment) / New logos
3. **LTV (Simple)** = (Average ACV × Gross Margin %) / Annual Churn Rate
   - Annual churn rate = 1 - Gross retention rate
4. **NRR-Adjusted LTV** = (Average ACV × Gross Margin %) × (NRR / Annual Churn Rate)
   - This captures expansion revenue in the LTV calculation
5. **LTV/CAC Ratio** (use blended CAC)
   - Green: > 5x | Yellow: 3-5x | Red: < 3x
6. **Marketing CAC Payback** (months) = Marketing CAC / (ACV × Gross Margin % / 12)
7. **Blended CAC Payback** (months) = Blended CAC / (ACV × Gross Margin % / 12)
8. **NRR-Adjusted Payback** = Payback adjusted for expansion (if NRR > 100%, payback accelerates after initial term)
9. **Marketing Efficiency Score** = (New ARR generated from segment last 12 months) / (Marketing spend allocated to segment)
   - Above 1.5x = efficient | 1.0-1.5x = acceptable | Below 1.0x = inefficient

**Advanced metrics (calculate if data is sufficient):**

10. **Contribution Margin per Customer** = (ACV × Gross Margin %) - Annual CS/Success cost per customer
11. **ARR per Marketing Dollar** = Segment ARR generated / Segment marketing spend
12. **Implied Customer Acquisition ROI** = (NRR-Adjusted LTV - Blended CAC) / Blended CAC × 100%
13. **Pipeline ROI** = (Segment pipeline generated) / (Marketing spend allocated to segment)

## Integration Tips

- **Salesforce/HubSpot CRM:** Pull customer count, ACV, and close date by segment using report builder. Filter by Segment/Industry field. Export CAC inputs as CSV. Feed into Claude prompt with calculated totals.
- **ChartMogul / Baremetrics / Paddle:** Export cohort retention and NRR data by customer segment tag. These tools produce the retention curves needed for accurate LTV modeling.
- **Looker / Tableau / Power BI:** Build a unit economics dashboard with segment as a dimension. Use this prompt's output to define the KPIs and thresholds to monitor (e.g., alert when segment LTV/CAC drops below 3x).
- **Google Sheets:** Run scenario modeling in a sheet. Columns = segments, rows = unit economics metrics. Use this prompt's output to populate the initial model, then iterate manually.
- **Notion / Confluence:** Store the segment prioritization matrix and reallocation rationale as a living document. Link to CRM segment definitions so ops team keeps segments consistent.
- **Quarterly CFO/CMO Review:** Run this analysis before every QBR. Track how LTV/CAC ratios change quarter-over-quarter to see the impact of budget shifts in real time.
- **Gong / Chorus:** Cross-reference win/loss data by segment to validate LTV assumptions. If a segment has high theoretical LTV but poor win rates, the model may be overstating returns.

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: I don't have clean segment-level data in my CRM — customers aren't consistently tagged by segment.**
Solution: Start with a sample analysis. Pull your 50 most recent customers, manually classify them by segment using firmographic data (LinkedIn, Clearbit, or ZoomInfo enrichment), and run the unit economics on this representative sample. Flag it as a sample analysis in your outputs. Use the findings to build a business case for cleaning up CRM segmentation going forward.

**Problem: My LTV calculations look unrealistically high (e.g., 50x+ LTV/CAC in one segment).**
Solution: This typically happens when churn is near zero (making the LTV formula produce very large numbers) or when NRR is very high. Apply a practical cap: use a 7-year customer lifetime maximum regardless of calculated retention, and double-check that your gross retention figure is tracking contract renewals, not just annual churn. Also verify you're using gross retention (not net) in the LTV denominator.

**Problem: The analysis shows I should dramatically cut SMB investment, but leadership is attached to SMB volume numbers.**
Solution: Present the unit economics alongside an "SMB efficiency improvement roadmap" — what would have to change about our SMB motion (CAC reduction, ACV increase, retention improvement) to make the LTV/CAC ratio acceptable? Frame reallocation as shifting investment toward a specific program experiment rather than abandoning the segment. A 90-day test with kill criteria is easier to get approved than permanent budget cuts.
