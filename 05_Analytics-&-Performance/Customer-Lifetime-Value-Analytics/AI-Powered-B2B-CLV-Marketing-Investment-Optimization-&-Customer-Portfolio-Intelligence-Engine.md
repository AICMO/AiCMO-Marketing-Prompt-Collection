## Overview
Transforms customer revenue, usage, and behavioral data into a CLV-stratified investment model that tells you exactly how much to spend acquiring, retaining, and expanding each customer segment — and which accounts deserve disproportionate investment because of their lifetime revenue potential. Use this when CAC is rising, board is pushing for efficiency, or your "best customers" aren't obvious from ACV alone.

**MODULE 1: CLV MODELING & SEGMENTATION MATRIX**

For each customer segment, calculate and present:

A. Simple CLV (for quick benchmarking):
   CLV = ACV × Gross Margin × (1 / Annual Churn Rate)

B. Expansion-Adjusted CLV (accounts for NRR growth):
   CLV = (ACV × GM) × [(1 + g) / (r − g)] where g = net expansion rate, r = discount rate + churn rate
   — This is the DCF-based model that accounts for both time value of money and account growth

C. Referral-Augmented CLV (total economic value including sourced pipeline):
   Total CLV = Expansion-Adjusted CLV + (Referral Rate × Average New Customer CLV × Average Referrals per Account)
   — Segments with high referral rates are systematically undervalued by standard CLV models

D. LTV:CAC Ratio Matrix:
   - Grade each segment: A (>5x), B (3-5x), C (2-3x), D (<2x — unsustainable)
   - Flag segments where CAC payback period exceeds 24 months as acquisition risk
   - Identify segments where LTV:CAC > 8x as potential under-investment opportunities

E. CLV Concentration Risk Analysis:
   - Lorenz curve for CLV distribution: what % of total portfolio CLV is held by top 10% of accounts?
   - Concentration risk score: <40% = diversified (healthy), 40-60% = moderate concentration, >60% = high risk
   - CLV at Risk (CVaR): if top 5 accounts churned, what % of total portfolio CLV is lost?

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: "I don't have segment-level churn and expansion data — my CRM doesn't break it down this way."**
Solution: Start with a rough segmentation using ACV bands as a proxy: run a Salesforce report of all Closed Won deals by ACV tier, cross-referenced with Closed Lost (Existing Business) and Expansion deals. For churn rates by segment, ask finance for the ARR waterfall by customer tier — most finance teams have this in their monthly close package even if marketing hasn't requested it. Even directional data (e.g., "we know Enterprise churns less than SMB") is enough to run the Quick Copy-Paste version and produce directionally correct investment guidance.

**Problem: "Our LTV:CAC for all segments is above 3x — the model doesn't flag any obvious reallocation."**
Solution: LTV:CAC > 3x across all segments is a good problem — but it often signals under-investment rather than optimal allocation. Re-run the model using the "aggressive ceiling" (CLV / 2) as your CAC benchmark. If current CAC is less than 50% of this ceiling in high-CLV segments, you are leaving growth on the table by under-investing in acquisition. Present the "CLV portfolio growth opportunity" framing to the board: "we could acquire $X additional CLV at below-ceiling CAC by investing $Y more in [segment]."

**Problem: "The CLV calculations produce very different numbers depending on whether I use simple vs. expansion-adjusted models — which do I trust?"**
Solution: Use expansion-adjusted CLV for any investment decisions involving accounts in their first 24 months — the simple model will undervalue high-expansion segments by 30-70%. Use simple CLV for quick executive communication and board summaries where simplicity matters more than precision. Always disclose your CLV calculation method when presenting — "This is expansion-adjusted CLV using a 12% discount rate" — so finance can validate your assumptions. If discount rate is contested, run sensitivity analysis at 8%, 12%, and 18% to show the range.
