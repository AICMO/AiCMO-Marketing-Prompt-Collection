## Overview
Every B2B buying decision is made by humans operating under cognitive constraints, emotional pressures, and organizational politics — not by rational actors following logical evaluation criteria. This engine builds a systematic buyer psychology research program that maps the cognitive biases, decision triggers, loss aversion patterns, and social dynamics that actually determine why deals are won, stalled, or lost. Use it to redesign your messaging, pricing architecture, proof strategy, and sales process around how buyers actually think — not how you wish they did.

**Output Example (Component 1 — Cognitive Bias Audit excerpt):**

**Bias: Status Quo Bias (Sunk Cost × Loss Aversion compound)**

How it manifests for VP RevOps: The RevOps analyst spent 6 months building the Excel capacity model. The VP has defended that model in two QBRs. Buying Revcast implicitly admits the current model is insufficient — which reads internally as "my team built something that didn't work." The cognitive cost isn't the $40K budget — it's the perceived indictment of past decisions.

Stage where strongest: Post-demo, during internal evaluation. The VP is your champion but can't fully commit because it means walking back the status quo they defended.

Countermeasure: Never attack the existing model. Frame Revcast as the "intelligence layer that makes your current model smarter" in early conversations. Only introduce the full-replacement narrative after you've built personal trust. In your ROI case, calculate the hours the analyst saves — this lets the VP position the purchase as "freeing my best analyst for strategic work" rather than "admitting the model failed."

Bias trap to avoid: Showing a side-by-side comparison that demonstrates how much the Excel model underperforms. This is factually correct and psychologically toxic. It triggers defensive commitment to the status quo. Instead: show what Revcast produces and let the prospect draw the comparison themselves.

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: Reps resist using psychological frameworks — "This feels manipulative."**
Solution: Reframe the entire framework internally as "friction reduction, not manipulation." The psychology research reveals where buyers are already feeling fear, confusion, and risk — the framework helps you address those real concerns proactively rather than exploit them. Run a 30-minute training session using the Loss Map: ask reps to recall a deal that went dark and map it against the loss categories. The exercise makes the framework feel like empathy, not manipulation, because it is.

**Problem: The buyer psychology profile doesn't match what our reps are actually experiencing in the field.**
Solution: The initial profile is a hypothesis, not a diagnosis. Run it through the reality test: take your last 10 lost deals and score each loss against the 8 cognitive biases (Component 1). Which bias appears most often? If "complexity aversion" is the top pattern but your current messaging focuses on loss aversion, the framework needs to be recalibrated. This discrepancy is valuable — it means your messaging is optimizing for the wrong psychological lever. Update the framework to prioritize the biases that are actually causing losses.

**Problem: The buying committee map is accurate for one segment but wrong for another (e.g., maps correctly for SMB but fails for enterprise).**
Solution: Segment size dramatically changes committee psychology. In SMB (under 100 employees), the economic buyer often has lower organizational risk tolerance and makes decisions faster because career consequences are more direct. In enterprise (500+), committee consensus dynamics dominate and every member is primarily avoiding blame rather than pursuing wins. Build separate buyer psychology profiles for each major ICP segment. Use the CRM trigger-type field to track which profile applies to which deal, and measure win rates separately by segment.
