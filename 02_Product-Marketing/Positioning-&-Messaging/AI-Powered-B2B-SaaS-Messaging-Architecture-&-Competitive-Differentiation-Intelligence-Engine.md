## Overview

Use this whenever you're launching a new product, repositioning against a competitor, entering a new market segment, or when win rates are declining because messaging has drifted from buyer reality.

OUTPUT STRUCTURE:

## 1. CATEGORY NARRATIVE
Three versions:
- BOLD (category-creating): Position us as the inventor of a new category. Use "Until now..." structure.
- SAFE (category-claiming): Plant our flag as the leader of an existing category. Use "The [Category] platform built for [specific segment]..." structure.
- CHALLENGER (category-disrupting): Attack the assumption that the existing category solves the real problem. Use "The old way of [X] was designed for a world that no longer exists..." structure.

Include: Which version to use in which context (enterprise sales vs. demand gen vs. AR briefings).

## 2. PRIMARY VALUE PROPOSITION
- Hero Statement (website H1, max 12 words): Outcome-first. Specific buyer. Quantified where possible.
- Subhead (25-35 words): Explains the mechanism and who it's for.
- Body (2-3 sentences): Expands on the promise with one credibility anchor.

A/B Test Variant: Provide one alternative H1 with a different emotional hook (e.g., fear of loss vs. aspiration).

## 3. THREE MESSAGING PILLARS
For each pillar:
- Pillar Name (2-4 words): The capability cluster
- Headline (one sentence): The promise this pillar makes
- Proof Point 1: Quantified customer outcome
- Proof Point 2: Product feature / mechanism that enables it
- Business Impact: CFO-friendly "so what" (revenue, cost, risk)
- Pillar vs. [INCUMBENT]: One sentence showing how our pillar beats theirs on this dimension

## 4. PERSONA MESSAGING MATRIX
For each of the three buyer roles:
- Primary Fear: What keeps them up at night
- Primary Aspiration: What success looks like for them
- Our Hook: The one-liner tailored to their worldview (use their language, not ours)
- Proof Point That Resonates: Which of our proof points maps to their metric
- Content That Converts: What type of content (ROI calculator, technical whitepaper, peer case study) moves them forward
- Words to Use / Avoid: 5 words that resonate, 3 to avoid

## 5. COMPETITIVE POSITIONING CARDS
For each competitor:
- Their Narrative Strength (acknowledge honestly)
- Their Positioning Vulnerability (where they're overextended or outdated)
- Our Wedge Statement (2 sentences: validate their strength, then pivot to our advantage)
- Trap Question (a discovery question that naturally surfaces their weakness without naming them)
- Landmine (a feature/requirement we should always put in RFPs that they struggle to meet)
- Never Say (phrases that trigger "sounds like [competitor]" in buyer's mind)

## 6. OBJECTION REFRAME LIBRARY
For each of the top 5 objections:
- Objection (verbatim as buyers say it)
- What They Really Mean (the underlying fear or hidden agenda)
- Reframe (flip the objection into evidence for choosing us)
- Proof Point to Deploy (specific data or story that closes the reframe)
- Escalation Path (if reframe doesn't work, what's the next move)

## 7. NARRATIVE SEQUENCES
- 30-Second Elevator Pitch (conversational, human, non-corporate)
- 2-Minute Demo Opener (hooks attention, establishes stakes before screen share)
- 5-Minute Executive Presentation Open (board/C-suite room, leads with business problem)
- Cold Outreach First Line (personalized hook template, fill-in-the-blank)

## 8. MESSAGE TESTING SCORECARD
Before launching, grade this messaging architecture against these criteria:
- [ ] Clarity: Can a smart 16-year-old understand the value prop?
- [ ] Specificity: Are there real numbers, not vague promises?
- [ ] Differentiation: Would this messaging also work for a competitor? (It shouldn't)
- [ ] Buyer-Centricity: Is the buyer the hero, not the product?
- [ ] Falsifiability: Can you prove each claim?
Rate each 1-5 and flag any that score below 4 with a suggested fix.

CONSTRAINTS:
- Never use: "leading," "best-in-class," "innovative," "revolutionary," "cutting-edge," "holistic," "seamless," "robust," "leverage," "synergy"
- Every claim must be either proven or flagged as [INSERT PROOF]
- Persona language must mirror how buyers actually describe their pain — use interview quotes format where possible
- Competitive content must be factually defensible — no FUD, no disparagement
- All outputs must be copy-paste ready for sales decks, website, and email

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: The messaging sounds like everyone else in our category — not differentiated**
*Fix:* Go back to the Unique Mechanism field. If you can't articulate a specific "how" that competitors can't replicate, you don't have a differentiator yet — you have a feature. Run customer interviews with the question "Why us and not [Competitor]?" Their answers are your real differentiators. Replace any adjective-based claims ("faster," "easier") with mechanism-based claims ("because we X, while competitors still Y").

**Problem: Sales team isn't adopting the new messaging**
*Fix:* The most common cause is messaging that sounds good in a document but feels unnatural spoken aloud. Test every section out loud before publishing. Record yourself delivering the 30-second elevator pitch — if it sounds like a press release, rewrite it. Run a 30-minute workshop where reps practice the elevator pitch and objection reframes with each other. Adoption follows practice, not reading.

**Problem: Messaging works for one persona but confuses others**
*Fix:* This is a sequencing problem. Your primary value proposition should be written for your champion (the person who builds the internal business case). Then the Persona Messaging Matrix handles translation for the other roles — you should never lead with the CFO message in a champion conversation. Check that your sales team knows to adapt the message by room, not use a one-size-fits-all script.
