# COMPETITIVE FRAMEWORKS TO APPLY

**Objection Handling:** Use the ACRC framework — Acknowledge (validate the concern), Clarify (get specifics), Reframe (shift the lens), Close (confirm the reframe landed).

**Discovery Questions:** Use the SPIN-inspired approach — questions that reveal Situation, surface Problems, amplify Implications, and build Need-Payoff.

**Kill Shots:** Apply narrative judo — take their claimed strength and show why, in the buyer's real-world context, it's actually a constraint or a liability. Always anchor to proof (G2 data, customer quotes, analyst research, functional demos).

**Comparison Framework:** Map dimensions to what buyers actually evaluate in vendor reviews — NOT your marketing speak. Use language buyers use in RFPs and procurement conversations.

## Troubleshooting

**Problem:** The battlecard is too long and reps won't read it.
**Solution:** Create a "rep version" (1 Notion page, scannable bullets, bold kill shots) and a "PMM version" (full context, evidence, update log). Auto-link from CRM so reps get the right one. Add a "TL;DR box" at the very top with 3 bullets: their pitch, your win angle, the one-liner that closes it.

**Problem:** Kill shots feel generic or the evidence is weak.
**Solution:** Run 5 win/loss interviews with customers who switched from this competitor. Ask: "What was the moment you knew [Competitor] wasn't right?" That language becomes your kill shots. G2 reviews with filters for "switched from [Competitor]" are also gold mines.

**Problem:** Battlecard goes stale within 3 months and reps lose trust in it.
**Solution:** Build a quarterly battlecard review into your PMM calendar. Create a Slack channel where reps can drop real-time intel ("heard this in a call today"). Tie battlecard freshness to a version number and "verified date" — reps will flag when it's outdated if you make it easy.
