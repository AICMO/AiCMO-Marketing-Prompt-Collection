## Troubleshooting

**Problem: We don't have access to paid analyst research, just public summaries.**
Solution: You don't need paid research for most of this engine. The highest-value signals come from free sources: analyst LinkedIn posts, blog posts on firm websites, webcast recordings on YouTube, conference session recordings, G2 category definitions and review trends, Gartner Peer Insights public reviews, analyst podcast appearances, and Twitter/X commentary threads. A systematic PMM can extract 80% of the competitive intelligence value from public sources in 3–4 hours per week using an AI agent to monitor and synthesize.

**Problem: Analysts are saying positive things about a competitor and we can't find an analyst quote supporting us.**
Solution: Don't manufacture quotes — instead, run Module 3's "Messaging Weaponization" starting from G2 reviews and Gartner Peer Insights customer reviews, which are public and directly buyer-sourced. Quote customer patterns rather than analyst opinions when analyst quotes favor competitors: "Customers in Gartner Peer Insights consistently say [Competitor] is hard to implement for mid-market companies" is often as powerful as an analyst saying it directly. Then run Module 4 to identify which analysts don't yet have a strong view of competitors — those are your best briefing targets.

**Problem: Our sales team doesn't use the analyst intelligence we create.**
Solution: The briefing format matters more than the content. The weekly signal digest must be ≤1 page, include an explicit "USE THIS IN A CALL THIS WEEK" action for every item, and land in their inbox or Slack channel Monday morning. Run a 30-day pilot: attach one analyst proof point to a specific competitive deal type (e.g., "Use this Forrester quote only in deals where prospect mentions [Competitor]"), track win rate, report back. Sales teams adopt tools that provably help them close — prove ROI on one proof point first, then expand.
