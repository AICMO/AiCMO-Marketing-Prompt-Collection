**D. IDC MarketScape Preparation Playbook**

Key differences from Forrester and how to prepare:

*IDC Methodology Distinctions:*
- IDC uses a bubble chart (not 2x2 matrix) where bubble position = Capabilities score and bubble size = Strategies score
- IDC's evaluation process is more interview-intensive and less RFI-driven than Forrester
- IDC analysts typically conduct 2-3 live interviews vs. Forrester's primary RFI process
- "Capabilities" in IDC = current product depth, customer results, ecosystem integrations
- "Strategies" in IDC = go-to-market approach, partner ecosystem, product vision, pricing flexibility
- IDC Market Presence criteria: minimum revenue thresholds vary by category but typically require $20M+ in category revenue to qualify as "Participant"

*IDC Interview Preparation:*
Format each of the 3 live IDC interviews differently:
- Interview 1 (Capabilities deep-dive): Live product walkthrough, customer evidence, integration ecosystem breadth, support model depth
- Interview 2 (Strategies focus): Executive Q&A on roadmap, pricing model, geographic strategy, partner ecosystem investments
- Interview 3 (Customer reference calls): IDC analyst speaks directly with 2-3 reference customers selected by vendor — pre-brief customers on likely topics

*IDC Evidence Standards:*
IDC values highly specific, numerically supported claims. For every capability claim, prepare:
- The feature/function name and release date
- Number of enterprise customers using it (by segment if available)
- Customer outcome metric (e.g., "average customer sees 23% reduction in [X]") backed by source
- Third-party validation (audit report, analyst quote, case study)

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: We submitted an RFI for a Forrester Wave and were not included in the final evaluation — no placement at all.**
Solution: This almost always means one of three things: (1) You fell below Forrester's minimum market presence threshold for the evaluation (usually a combination of revenue scale, customer count, and geographic coverage — Forrester publishes minimum inclusion criteria for most Waves). Request a debrief with the lead analyst to understand specifically which threshold you missed. If it's revenue, there's no shortcut; if it's customer count or geography, you may be able to address it by the next cycle. (2) Your product doesn't sufficiently address the criteria scope — Forrester evaluates products against the full criterion set, and if your product covers only 60% of the scope, you may be excluded as out-of-scope. Request a 30-minute advisory call to understand which criteria fall outside your coverage. (3) You were late to the RFI submission or missed the participation deadline. Forrester has strict deadlines; set calendar alerts 30 days before the expected RFI opening.

**Problem: We earned a Strong Performer or Contender placement but our sales team isn't using the report — no visible impact on pipeline.**
Solution: The activation failure is usually one of three causes: (1) Sales reps don't know how to use analyst placements in a competitive context — run a 30-minute enablement session showing exactly when to share the report (best: when a prospect mentions a competitor or asks "how do you compare to X?"), what to say when sharing it, and how to frame a Contender/Strong Performer placement positively against a competitor's Leader status. (2) The placement asset format is wrong — a 40-page PDF is unusable in sales conversations. Create a 1-page "What Our Placement Means" document that excerpts the 2-3 most relevant criteria where you scored highest and frame each in "what this means for your evaluation." (3) The sales team fundamentally doesn't trust analyst reports because they've seen prospects dismiss them. Track actual win rates in analyst-cited vs. non-cited deals for one quarter before drawing conclusions — the data usually shows a lift that converts skeptical sales reps.

**Problem: Our reference customers agreed to participate but their survey responses were weaker than expected — we got a lower score on the Customer Success criteria than anticipated.**
Solution: This is a preparation failure, not a customer satisfaction failure. Before the next evaluation cycle: (1) Select reference customers more selectively — require a minimum NPS score of 8+ and a documented outcome metric before adding them to the Wave reference pool. (2) Brief each reference customer with the specific questions the analyst survey will ask. Forrester's surveys for most Waves include product capability ratings, support quality, vendor relationship, and outcome achievement — walk customers through each category in a 30-minute prep call. (3) Use customer success health score data to identify your highest-outcome customers (those who achieved ROI within 6 months and have actively expanded) as first-priority references, not just the most enthusiastic advocates who may lack quantified outcomes. (4) After each evaluation cycle, debrief 2-3 reference customers to understand what questions surprised them and update your prep guide accordingly.
