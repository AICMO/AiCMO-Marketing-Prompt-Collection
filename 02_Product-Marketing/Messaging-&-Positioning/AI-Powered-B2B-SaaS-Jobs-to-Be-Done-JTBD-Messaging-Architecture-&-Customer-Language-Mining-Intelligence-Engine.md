# AI-Powered B2B SaaS Jobs-to-Be-Done (JTBD) Messaging Architecture & Customer Language Mining Intelligence Engine

**Difficulty:** Advanced | **Time:** 20-30 min | **Tags:** jobs-to-be-done, jtbd, messaging, positioning, customer-language-mining, voice-of-customer, b2b-saas, product-marketing, message-market-fit, switch-interviews

## Overview

This engine translates raw customer language — from sales calls, win/loss interviews, review sites, and community discussions — into a complete Jobs-to-Be-Done (JTBD) messaging architecture that drives message-market fit across every buyer touchpoint. Use it when your messaging feels generic, when sales reps paraphrase product features instead of buyer outcomes, or when you need to rebuild positioning from actual purchase psychology rather than internal assumptions.

   - Primary value proposition (1 sentence, outcome-focused, using customer language)
   - Headline variants (5 options using JTBD language)

PHASE 1 — JTBD RESEARCH SYNTHESIS (Days 1-14)

A. Primary Job Statement Construction

The Jobs-to-Be-Done framework requires a precise job statement format:
[Situation/Actor] + [Motivation Verb] + [Object of Motivation] + [Clarifying Context]

Correct example: "When I am a General Counsel at a company three months post-acquisition, I need to identify every contract obligation that survived the deal so I can brief the board on integration risk without getting surprised by unknown liabilities at close."

Incorrect example (too generic): "Legal teams want better contract visibility."

For each ICP segment, construct 3-5 Primary Job Statements organized by hierarchy:

Job Hierarchy:
- Big Job (overarching outcome buyer is trying to own): e.g., "Be known as the GC who prevents contract surprises — not the one explaining them after they happen"
- Functional Jobs (specific tasks that unlock the Big Job): e.g., "Find all auto-renewal clauses before they trigger without warning"; "Produce a complete contract risk report within 48 hours of an audit request"
- Micro-Jobs (granular tasks that must work for the functional job to be possible): e.g., "Get a single source of truth for all contract metadata instead of hunting 4 SharePoint folders"

Quality test for each job statement: Can you tell a specific story about a buyer that ends with them starting to search for a solution? If the job statement could apply to any company in any context, it is not specific enough.

B. Four Forces Mapping — Switch Interview Analysis

Apply the Four Forces framework to understand buyer psychology at the moment of switching. The goal is to reconstruct the timeline: something changed, making inaction intolerable for the first time.

FORCE 1: Push of the Current Situation
What is actively making the buyer's current situation intolerable? These are energy sources — acute moments of failure, embarrassment, cost, or risk that finally force action. The switch almost never happens gradually; there is usually a precipitating incident.

Extraction method: In Gong/call transcripts, search for phrases containing:
- "The reason we're looking now is..."
- "What finally made us do something was..."
- "We had an incident where..."
- "My CEO/board/auditor said..."
- "We lost [client/money/deal] because..."

Build a Push Trigger Table:
| Push Trigger Theme | Verbatim Quote Example | Frequency (calls/interviews) | Emotional Intensity (1-5) | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Missed auto-renewal | "We missed a $2M vendor renewal because no one owned the date and it auto-renewed for another 3 years" | 8/22 win interviews | 5 | Clozd win interviews |
| Post-M&A chaos | "We acquired a company and nobody knew what contracts they had — we found 3 change of control clauses by accident, two months in" | 4/12 Switch interviews | 5 | JTBD Switch interviews |
| Audit failure | "Our external auditor asked for all contracts with revenue recognition impact and we couldn't produce the list in 48 hours" | 6/94 G2 reviews | 4 | G2 reviews |

FORCE 2: Pull of the New Solution
What is the "promised land" vision that attracted the buyer toward a new solution? These are the specific outcomes they imagined — or heard from peers — about what life looks like after switching. Pull is almost always aspirational and social: buyers do not just want to fix a problem, they want to be seen differently.

Extraction method: In win interviews, search for:
- "What we imagined it would be like was..."
- "I heard from [peer/community] that they can now..."
- "The dream was that I could just..."
- "What made us confident was seeing [customer/case study] that..."

Build a Pull Aspiration Table:
| Pull Aspiration | Verbatim Quote | Associated Big Job | Messaging Implication |
|---|---|---|---|
| Effortless board readiness | "I just want to pull up every active obligation for any contract in 30 seconds, not three days of searching" | Be the GC who prevents surprises | Headline candidate: "Your board asked for a contract risk summary. Here it is in 30 seconds." |
| Credibility with the CEO | "I want to be the person who already has the answer when the CEO calls — not the one saying 'let me get back to you'" | GC as trusted risk advisor | Value prop element: "Answer the CFO's contract question in real-time instead of two weeks later" |

FORCE 3: Anxiety About the New Solution
What fears prevent buyers from moving forward even when they know the status quo is unacceptable? These are the conversion killers — objections that never get voiced but silently block decisions.

Common B2B SaaS switching anxieties (validate against your data):
- Implementation risk: "What if this takes 6 months and we get nothing done?"
- Data migration: "What happens to our existing 8,000 contracts — do they need to be re-entered manually?"
- Adoption: "Legal teams are notoriously resistant to new tools — will anyone actually use it?"
- Job security: "If this automates what my team does, does it make us less valuable?"
- Vendor risk: "What if this company gets acquired or goes under in 18 months?"

For each anxiety identified, define the specific counter-message — the proof point, social proof element, implementation guarantee, or reframe that neutralizes the fear:

| Anxiety | Verbatim Evidence | Counter-Message | Counter-Message Proof Point |
|---|---|---|---|
| "Implementation will take too long" | "Our last legal tech project took 8 months and we still haven't fully adopted it" | "Live in 2 weeks, fully adopted in 30 days — or we extend your contract free" | "Average time-to-first-value across 200+ deployments: 11 days" |
| "My team won't use it" | "We bought a system two years ago and people just went back to email" | "98% of users are self-trained in under 2 hours — designed for lawyers, not engineers" | Customer quote from GC at [Company] + G2 "Ease of Use" 9.4/10 |

FORCE 4: Habit of the Present
What inertia keeps buyers in their current situation even when they are dissatisfied? These are the "good enough" rationalizations that delay decisions — and the status quo you are competing with more often than any named competitor.

Examples:
- "We built a SharePoint system that works — it is not great but people know it"
- "We just hired a paralegal to manage this — we will see if that helps before spending more"
- "We have lived with this problem for 2 years; we can live with it another quarter"

Counter-habit messages must make the cost of inaction concrete and quantifiable:
- "Every quarter you manage contracts in SharePoint costs you [X hours] in legal team time and [Y risk exposure] in missed renewal penalties — this math compounds annually"

PHASE 2 — CUSTOMER LANGUAGE MINING (Days 7-14)

A. Language Mining Methodology by Source

SOURCE 1: Gong/Chorus Call Mining
Use AI to analyze discovery call transcripts. Submit this extraction prompt to your transcript analysis tool or Claude:

"In these [N] discovery call transcripts, identify and extract:
1. Every phrase where a buyer describes their current situation in their own words (proactively — not responding to our leading questions)
2. Every phrase where a buyer describes what success looks like in their own terms
3. Every phrase where a buyer describes a specific failure, incident, or consequence of their current approach
4. Every phrase where a buyer compares our solution to their status quo or to competitors
5. Every phrase that contains an emotional modifier: frustrating, embarrassing, stressful, worried, relieved, exciting, scary

Extract only direct quotes — do not paraphrase. Tag each with: call ID, speaker role, and emotional valence (positive/negative/neutral)."

SOURCE 2: G2/Capterra Review Mining
Submit this analysis prompt against your exported reviews:

"Analyze these [N] software reviews and extract:
1. The specific job or outcome the reviewer was trying to achieve (not the features they mention — the progress they were seeking)
2. 'Before state' language — how they describe their situation before using the product
3. 'After state' language — how they describe their situation after using the product
4. Decision trigger phrases — what made them look for a solution
5. Metaphors or analogies reviewers use to explain the value they received

Extract only direct quotes with source review ID. Do not paraphrase. Tag each with: the JTBD job category it belongs to."

SOURCE 3: Community and Dark Social Mining
Manual and AI-assisted search of relevant communities (Slack, Reddit, LinkedIn groups, industry forums):

Search strings to use:
- "[Product category] + frustration OR problem OR nightmare OR manual"
- "How do you [core job to be done] at your company"
- "Looking for recommendations on [specific problem area]"
- "We just switched from [status quo] to [category]"
- "Anyone else dealing with [problem]"

B. Customer Language Dictionary Construction

After mining all sources, build the Customer Language Dictionary — the definitive vocabulary that must appear in all external messaging:

Format (target 30+ entries):
| Internal Language (What We Say) | Customer Language (What They Say) | Emotional Weight | Where to Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| "Contract lifecycle management" | "Keeping track of what we promised and when it is due" | Low engagement — too abstract | Avoid in headlines; OK in educational content |
| "AI extraction and classification" | "It reads the contract so I do not have to" | High delight and relatability | Homepage tagline, ad creative |
| "Obligation management" | "Making sure nothing falls through the cracks" | Medium anxiety, universal resonance | Email subject lines, nurture sequences |
| "Metadata normalization" | "Getting everything into one place where you can actually find it" | High relief | Value prop bullets, onboarding emails |
| "Automated contract ingestion" | "Dragging contracts in instead of retyping them" | Medium relief | Feature pages, trial onboarding copy |

Dictionary governance rules:
- Every entry must cite its source (call ID, review ID, or interview transcript)
- Internal language terms are flagged — use only in technical documentation, not buyer-facing copy
- Update the dictionary every 6 months by re-running the mining protocol

PHASE 3 — MESSAGING ARCHITECTURE CONSTRUCTION (Days 10-21)

A. Primary Value Proposition

Using customer language from the dictionary and JTBD job statements, construct the primary value proposition using this formula:

"[Product Name] helps [ICP] [achieve specific functional outcome in customer language] so they can [emotional or social job outcome] — without [primary switching anxiety or status quo habit]."

Test this candidate against four gates before accepting it as canonical:
1. Language gate: Does it use customer language or internal product language? (If "optimize," "leverage," or "AI-powered" appear without customer language equivalents, rewrite)
2. Job gate: Does it reflect the Big Job, not just a micro-job?
3. Social/emotional gate: Does it include an emotional or social outcome, not just a functional one?
4. Anxiety gate: Does it neutralize or acknowledge the most common switching anxiety?

B. Headline Testing Matrix

Build 10 headline variants for A/B testing, organized by JTBD angle:

| Headline | JTBD Angle | Target Persona | Emotional Driver | Test Priority |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| "The contract clause your team missed just became a $3M problem. Let us fix that." | Push trigger — incident | GC post-incident | Fear/urgency | Test 1 (highest push energy) |
| "Every obligation. Every deadline. Every risk. In one place." | Functional job — visibility | VP Legal Ops | Control/confidence | Test 2 |
| "Your board asked for a contract risk summary. Here it is in 30 seconds." | Social job — board readiness | GC/CLO | Status/credibility | Test 3 |
| "What if your legal team had time to do legal work instead of hunting contracts?" | Emotional job — time reclaimed | GC/CHRO | Relief | Test 4 |
| "The compliance audit is next month. Your contract database is SharePoint." | Push trigger — anxiety | CCO | Fear/urgency | Test 5 |
| "500 contracts. 12 auto-renewals this quarter. Do you know which ones?" | Quantified push trigger | VP Legal Ops | Anxiety/curiosity | Test 6 |
| "The GC who knows what every contract says before the CEO asks." | Social job aspirational | CLO/GC | Aspiration/identity | Test 7 |
| "From 2-week contract searches to 30-second answers." | Before/after velocity | VP Legal Ops | Relief/efficiency | Test 8 |
| "Your M&A due diligence just found 47 contracts the sellers forgot to mention." | Specific incident trigger | GC/M&A counsel | Urgency/specificity | Test 9 |
| "Built for General Counsel who cannot afford to be surprised." | Persona identity headline | GC | Confidence/identity | Test 10 |

C. Persona Messaging Cards

Build one card per ICP segment for use in sales decks, SDR outreach, and ABM content:

PERSONA CARD 1: General Counsel — Enterprise (2,000+ employees)

PHASE 4 — MESSAGE TESTING AND MARKET FIT MEASUREMENT (Days 21-45)

A. Three-Environment Message Test Design

TEST 1: Homepage Headline A/B Test
- Control: Current homepage headline
- Treatment A: Push trigger headline (incident-focused, Test 1 from matrix)
- Treatment B: Social job headline (board readiness, Test 3 from matrix)
- Treatment C: Functional job headline (visibility, Test 2 from matrix)
- Primary metric: Demo request conversion rate
- Minimum sample: 500 unique visitors per variant (use paid traffic to reach sample size in under 30 days)
- Decision threshold: ≥20% relative lift over control = declare winner and roll out
- Secondary metrics: Time on page, scroll depth, bounce rate

TEST 2: Sales Discovery Call Language Test
- Experiment design: Split sales team by rep (not by deal) — 50% use JTBD customer language opening; 50% continue with current feature-led opening
- Primary tracking metrics:
  - Time to "buyer engagement moment" in call (when does the buyer first ask a substantive question or lean in?)
  - Discovery-to-demo-show rate
  - Discovery-to-qualified-opportunity conversion rate
- Measurement tool: Gong scoring — have PMM listen to 20 calls per condition and score engagement level by minute mark
- Run for 6 weeks minimum, 30+ calls per condition before reading results

TEST 3: Outbound Email Subject Line JTBD Test
- Send 5-variant email to cold prospect list (segment: GCs at 1,000+ employee companies, sourced from Apollo/Clay)
- Variant 1 (control): Feature-led subject line (current language)
- Variant 2: Push trigger subject line — "The contract clause your team missed just became a $3M problem"
- Variant 3: Social job subject line — "The GC who knows what every contract says before the CEO asks"
- Variant 4: Quantified push — "How many auto-renewals does your company have this quarter?"
- Variant 5: Before/after velocity — "From 2-week contract searches to 30 seconds"
- Minimum list size: 300 per variant (1,500 total)
- Measure: Open rate + click-through rate + reply rate
- Win condition: ≥15% relative lift in meaningful engagement (click or reply) vs. control

B. Message-Market Fit Score

Track these leading and lagging indicators monthly after launch:

LEADING INDICATORS (you are winning):
- Sales reps start using customer language unprompted in discovery calls (measure via Gong keyword tracking)
- Buyers quote your headline or value prop back to you in demo calls
- Inbound leads describe their problem using your messaging language in form fills
- Homepage time-on-page increases (buyers reading, not bouncing)
- SDR reply rates increase on JTBD-language outbound sequences

LAGGING INDICATORS (proof of message-market fit):
- Homepage conversion rate improvement ≥25% from JTBD message rollout
- Demo-to-qualified-opportunity conversion rate improvement ≥15%
- Win rate improvement ≥5 percentage points within 6 months
- Deal velocity improvement (days from first meeting to closed-won) ≥10%

WEAK MESSAGE-MARKET FIT SIGNALS (still iterating):
- Buyers ask "so what does this actually do?" after reading the homepage
- Sales reps revert to feature-feature language within 3 weeks of training
- Ad click-through rates are flat or declining (messaging does not match search intent)
- Demo discovery requires 45+ minutes for buyer to articulate the core value

---

CONSTRAINTS:
- All message candidates must be tested against at least 10 verbatim customer quotes before becoming canonical — internal consensus does not override real buyer language
- Job statements must describe a specific situation that triggered switching, not a generic ongoing pain — the test: "did something change that made inaction no longer acceptable?"
- Every Customer Language Dictionary entry must cite its source (call ID, review ID, interview) — unattributed language is inadmissible as messaging evidence
- Message test decisions must use defined statistical thresholds — gut instinct overrides are documented and post-mortemed
- Persona messaging cards are reviewed by at least 3 sales reps before going into use — reps must confirm "this is how buyers describe their problem"

OUTPUT FORMAT:
Deliver as a production-ready messaging system with:
1. 3-5 JTBD Primary Job Statements with situation, motivation, success metric, and story test
2. Four Forces Switch Psychology Map with verbatim quote evidence, frequency data, and counter-messages for each force
3. Customer Language Dictionary (30+ entries) with source citations and usage guidance
4. Primary Value Proposition (JTBD-optimized) with 3 persona-specific variants
5. Headline Testing Matrix (10 variants) with JTBD angle, emotional driver, and A/B test priority
6. Persona Messaging Cards (one per ICP segment) in sales-ready format
7. Message Testing Playbook with sample sizes, metrics, decision thresholds, and iteration protocol

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: We do not have Gong or call recordings — only CRM deal notes written by reps in internal product language, not buyer language.**
Solution: Run a "Language Extraction Sprint" before any call recordings are available. Schedule 5 structured 30-minute sessions with your top 3 salespeople using the Switch Interview question sequence: "Tell me about the last deal you closed where the buyer was coming from a spreadsheet or status quo approach — not actively shopping. What specific thing did they tell you was the event that finally made them look? What words did they use to describe that moment?" AEs remember buyer language from emotionally charged moments even when they do not record calls. Supplement with 30 minutes of G2 review mining using the AI extraction prompt from Phase 2 — reviews are the second-richest source of switch trigger language and are freely available.

**Problem: Our JTBD Job Statements feel generic — "VP HR wants to manage headcount better" — and they are not producing better messaging than what we had before.**
Solution: Generic job statements describe the product category, not the specific crisis point that triggers switching. Apply the story test: can you tell a specific story with a real character, a specific incident, and a moment of intolerable frustration that ends with this buyer starting to search? If the answer is no, the job statement is still too abstract. Return to the Four Forces Push Triggers — the specific incident that finally broke the camel's back is almost always the raw material for a precise job statement. The difference: Generic — "VP HR wants to reduce bench waste." Specific JTBD — "When my CEO asked me why utilization dropped to 68% last quarter and I had to say I did not know until it already happened, I knew I could not go into another board meeting that way." That incident becomes the job statement, the headline candidate, and the sales opener simultaneously.

**Problem: Sales team resists switching from feature-to-outcome language — reps say buyers want to know what the product does, not how it makes them feel.**
Solution: Run a controlled experiment before debating the methodology. Ask 2-3 willing reps to open discovery calls with the JTBD push trigger narrative for 4 weeks, tracking in Gong the exact minute when the buyer first asks a substantive question (leaning in) versus politely waiting for a slide to change. Compare this "buyer engagement minute" against calls using feature-first opening. In consistent testing across B2B SaaS, buyers engage 40-60% earlier in discovery when they hear their own problem language reflected back — versus a feature walkthrough where they listen politely until they can ask "what does it cost." Show the data, not the framework. Frame the experiment as "testing which language buyers respond to" — not "your current approach is wrong."
