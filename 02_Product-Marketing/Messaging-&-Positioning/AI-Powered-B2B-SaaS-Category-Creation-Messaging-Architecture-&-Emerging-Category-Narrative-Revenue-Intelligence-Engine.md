## Troubleshooting

**Problem: The category name tests poorly with buyers — they don't understand what it means without explanation**
Solution: A category name that requires explanation isn't ready. Run a 5-second test: show 10 target buyers the name and ask "what does this product do?" without additional context. If fewer than 6/10 arrive at the right general domain, the name is too abstract. Return to Section 1 and optimize for buyer-intuitive candidates over internally beloved ones. The best category names feel obvious in hindsight — not clever.

**Problem: Buyers respond to the category narrative with "that sounds like [existing category] with AI bolted on"**
Solution: The Problem Narrative (Section 2) is not doing sufficient work on the inflection point. Add more specificity to the failure cost quantification and make the inflection point tied to a specific, datable shift (e.g., "when [major industry event or technology shift] happened in [year]"). Buyers accept a new category when they can place the exact moment the old category became inadequate. Abstract critiques of legacy solutions invite skepticism; precise, dated critiques build credibility.

**Problem: The sales team defaults to product feature selling even after category training**
Solution: This is a sales methodology problem, not a messaging problem. The category narrative must be embedded in deal qualification and discovery process — not just enablement content. Work with sales leadership to add category-opening questions to discovery call frameworks ("What's your current [legacy category] approach?" followed by "What's the cost of that approach given [specific macro shift]?"). Use Gong to identify the 3 reps who naturally open with category narrative and turn their calls into training assets. Certification on category selling — not just category knowledge — is required for this to stick.
