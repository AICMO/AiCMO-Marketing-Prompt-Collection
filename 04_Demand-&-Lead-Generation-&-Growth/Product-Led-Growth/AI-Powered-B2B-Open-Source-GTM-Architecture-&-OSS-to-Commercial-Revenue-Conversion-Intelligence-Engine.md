## Integration Tips

**GitHub:**
- Pin a "Getting Started" and "Enterprise Features" post in GitHub Discussions — these become the top-of-funnel content your highest-intent visitors discover
- Use GitHub Releases to publish structured changelogs that can be auto-formatted for your blog, newsletter, and social posts — one source of truth, five distribution channels
- GitHub Actions Marketplace: publish a free GitHub Action that uses a core feature of your OSS project — this is top-of-funnel content that surfaces in developer workflows directly

**Community Analytics (Orbit, Common Room, Pocus):**
- Connect GitHub, Discord, npm downloads, and CRM into a unified community graph — identify "community qualified leads" by scoring based on depth of engagement (contributor > active Discord member > star-er > newsletter subscriber)
- Set up automated alerts when a contact from a target account domain performs a high-intent action (stars repo, visits pricing page, asks Discord question about enterprise deployment)

**CRM Integration (HubSpot/Salesforce):**
- Create a "Community Member" contact source to track OSS-to-CRM pipeline attribution separately from paid demand gen
- Build a "Developer Champion" contact role in your deal object — track when a closed enterprise deal had a community member as champion to measure community's sales influence rate

**Product Telemetry (PostHog, Segment, Amplitude):**
- Implement opt-in anonymous telemetry that captures deployment counts, feature usage, and version — this data becomes your "OSS health dashboard" and feeds enterprise upsell signals
- When users opt into telemetry, offer a personalized "your usage summary" email at 30 and 90 days — this is your first commercial touchpoint with context the developer cares about

**Documentation (Docusaurus, GitBook, Mintlify):**
- Install Koala, Clearbit Reveal, or RB2B to identify which companies are reading your enterprise-feature documentation — route these to SDRs as warm signals
- Add "Discuss this page on Discord" CTAs on documentation pages where enterprise evaluation questions commonly arise — moves commercial conversations into a monitored channel

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: GitHub stars growing but zero commercial pipeline from OSS**
*Root cause:* Usually one of three issues: (a) no telemetry to identify who is using the product beyond stars, (b) no commercial content path on the journey from docs to pricing page, (c) enterprise features too weak relative to the free tier — buyers see no reason to pay.
*Solution:* Audit your docs-to-trial funnel by installing Koala or RB2B on your docs site to identify visiting companies. Check your docs structure — is there a clear "scaling to production" section that naturally surfaces enterprise features? Run a 5-person user interview with companies that use the OSS but didn't convert to commercial to identify the actual barrier.

**Problem: Competitor forks our project or clones core features as fully open source**
*Root cause:* Your commercial moat is too thin — the OSS version's commercial features are too easy to replicate, or your licensing doesn't protect them adequately.
*Solution:* Evaluate Business Source License (BSL) or Server Side Public License (SSPL) for commercial protection (with awareness of community trust implications). More importantly: double down on community trust and developer relationships — a developer community that believes in your company is harder to fork than code. Publish an "OSS sustainability" blog post explaining your licensing decision and why commercial revenue funds the OSS project. Companies like HashiCorp, Elasticsearch, and MongoDB navigated this transition — study their playbooks.

**Problem: Contributors are active but none of them work at enterprise-size companies**
*Root cause:* Your OSS project may be optimized for hobbyists or small startups rather than enterprise deployment patterns — or your contributor onboarding doesn't make it easy for senior engineers at large companies to contribute.
*Solution:* Explicitly invite enterprise engineers to contribute enterprise-relevant features (SSO integrations, audit log schemas, Kubernetes operator patterns). Create a "Good First Issue for Enterprise" tag in GitHub Issues. Reach out to engineers at known enterprise ICP companies who star the repo — offer to co-write a case study or architecture review with them. Enterprise engineers want recognition for their contributions at work, not just GitHub points.
