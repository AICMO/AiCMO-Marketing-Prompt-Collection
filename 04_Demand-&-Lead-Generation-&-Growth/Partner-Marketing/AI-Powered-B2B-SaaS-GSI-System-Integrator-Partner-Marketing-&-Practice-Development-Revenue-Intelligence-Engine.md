Designs and operationalizes a complete Global System Integrator (GSI) and regional System Integrator (SI) partner marketing program that transforms consulting firm relationships into a repeatable partner-sourced pipeline engine — creating joint practice development investments, co-branded thought leadership, partner enablement content, and co-sell campaign architecture that generates enterprise deals through SI-led implementations. Use this when your software sells into enterprise accounts where consulting firms lead technology selection and implementation, when you have SI relationships that exist on paper but generate no pipeline, or when you want to build the "preferred partner" status with 2–3 strategic consulting firms before competitors do.

**Section 1: SI Practice Business Case & Joint Investment Framework**

*The SI Revenue Model Analysis:*

Enterprise SaaS implementations typically generate $3–$8 in SI services revenue for every $1 of software ARR (the "services multiplier"). Calculate the total billable services opportunity for each target SI: (number of your potential enterprise clients in the SI's existing client base) × (average implementation fee) + (average annual managed services / ongoing advisory retainer per account) = total addressable SI services revenue that your software creates. This number — presented in a joint business case — is the single most powerful argument for why an SI should invest in practice development. Quantify it specifically for each GSI. For example: "We estimate Deloitte's Finance Transformation practice serves approximately 400 Fortune 1000 clients who are potential [Your Product] buyers. **Section 4: Co-Sell Campaign Execution**

Design the active co-sell motions that generate partner-sourced pipeline from SI whitespace accounts:

*Whitespace Account Analysis and Activation:*
Step 1 — Account overlap analysis: Export your "target account" list from your CRM (accounts you have identified as ideal customers who are not yet customers). Cross-reference with each SI partner's known client list (request this from your SI alliance contact — most SIs will share a sanitized list of their top 200–500 accounts in exchange for a reciprocal account map). Step 2 — Account prioritization scoring: Score each overlap account on four factors: (1) SI relationship strength (do they have a partner/director-level relationship or just a junior engagement?), (2) your ICP fit score (how closely does the account match your ideal customer profile?), (3) buying trigger signals (recent job postings in your software's function, executive changes in the target department, or third-party intent data showing active research), and (4) competitor exposure (is the account currently using a competitor that the SI has historically displaced?). Prioritize the top 30 accounts per SI partner for immediate co-sell activation.

Step 3 — SI account team activation: For each of the top 30 accounts, prepare a "client briefing kit" for the SI account team: (a) a 1-page brief on why this client needs your software NOW (specific business problem + buying trigger evidence), (b) 3 pre-written email templates the SI account team can send to their executive contacts to introduce your software organically, and (c) a "discovery question" guide — 5 questions the SI consultant can ask during their next client engagement to uncover pain that your software solves.

Step 4 — Joint account planning: For the top 10 accounts per SI where the relationship and opportunity align, conduct a joint account planning session with the SI partner account executive. Use a shared 1-page account plan template that defines: the target buying committee by name, the specific business problem to address, the entry point (which current project or engagement creates a natural opening for your software), and the SI + Your Company joint "territory" on the account.

*Deal Registration and Co-Sell Mechanics:*
Design a frictionless deal registration system that SI partners will actually use:

Registration mechanism: Single-page form (in your partner portal or via email to your channel ops team) where SI registers: account name, primary executive contact, opportunity description, estimated ARR, and projected close date. Registration must take under 5 minutes or partners won't do it.

Response SLA: Your team acknowledges registration within 4 business hours, confirms acceptance (or flags conflict if there's a direct deal in progress), and assigns a co-sell resource within 24 hours. SI partners will stop registering deals if the response time is more than 2 business days.

Co-sell support package: Upon deal registration, SI partner receives: (a) dedicated pre-sales support — a named solutions engineer who will participate in client demonstrations, (b) executive sponsorship option — the option to request an executive briefing from your VP/C-suite for deals over $250K ARR, (c) competitive intelligence briefing — current battle card and objection handling guidance specific to competitors the client is evaluating, and (d) commercial terms flexibility — deal registration enables SI to offer a client commercial terms (e.g., favorable multi-year pricing) that wouldn't be available through direct procurement.

Deal protection: Once registered, the account is protected for 90 days (renewable if active opportunity). If your direct sales team is also working the account, define the rules of engagement: who owns the client relationship, who is on the calls, how commission is split. Ambiguity here destroys SI trust faster than any other issue.

*MDF Campaign Co-Funding Program:*
Design the MDF allocation and execution framework:

MDF eligibility: Tier 2 and Tier 3 SI partners receive MDF allocation based on prior quarter pipeline registered and certified consultant count. MDF is distributed quarterly, with 50% up-front and 50% upon proof of performance.

Approved MDF use cases and co-investment rates:
- Executive roundtable dinner: You fund 60% of event cost, SI funds 40%
- LinkedIn Ads targeting SI's client whitespace accounts: You fund 50%, SI funds 50% (SI provides the audience list, you provide the ad creative and budget)
- Co-branded content production (design, printing, translation): You fund 70%, SI funds 30% in internal resource time
- Conference sponsorship at SI-hosted client conferences: You fund 80%, SI funds hosting relationship and introductions
- Certification training events: You fund 100% up to $15K per event, SI funds consultant time

MDF proof of performance: SIs must submit within 30 days of campaign completion: invoices, post-event report (attendee list, deal opportunities created), and CRM evidence of pipeline generated. Unsubmitted proof = clawback of 50% MDF. This sounds harsh — but SIs that don't complete proof of performance reports aren't actually running the programs.

*SI Partner Scorecard (tracked monthly, reviewed quarterly):*

KPI 3: Co-sell win rate (win rate on deals with active SI co-sell support) — benchmark: typically 25–40% higher than direct-only win rate
KPI 4: Certified consultant count (active certifications across all SI partner tiers) — target: 50 certified consultants within 18 months across Tier 1–2 SI partners
KPI 5: Active implementations (SI-led implementations in progress or completed in trailing 12 months) — this is the leading indicator of future pipeline
KPI 6: Time-to-close on SI-sourced vs. direct deals — SI-sourced deals at enterprise level typically close faster because the SI has already built trust and accelerated the internal business case

*The "Shadow Implementation" Problem and How to Solve It:*
The most common failure mode in SI relationships: SI consultants implement your software but don't register the deal, take the software credit, or acknowledge your company's role — because they want to protect their client relationship and don't want you independently reaching into their account. Address this proactively:

Detection: Implement a quarterly "unregistered implementation audit" — your CS team identifies all new enterprise accounts where an SI consultant email is on the account (visible in support tickets, implementation contacts, or project documentation), then cross-references against deal registration records. Any SI-implemented account without a registered deal is a shadow implementation.

Resolution (non-confrontational): When you discover a shadow implementation, do NOT send a legal letter or threaten to revoke partner status. Instead, invite the SI practice leader to a "joint customer success review" for the account. Frame it as: "We want to make sure [Client Name] is getting maximum value from our partnership." In the meeting, propose formalizing the relationship with a deal registration (retroactively) so both teams can provide the client with better coordinated support. Most SI partners will comply when the ask is framed as being for the client's benefit.

Prevention: The real solution is making deal registration so valuable that SIs want to register deals. When registered deals get faster pre-sales support, better commercial terms, and co-sell resources — and unregistered implementations get none of that — the incentive structure solves the problem.

## Troubleshooting

**Problem 1: SI partners sign agreements and attend kickoffs but generate zero pipeline after 6 months.**
Root cause: SI consultants don't recommend software they haven't personally seen solve a client problem. Without certified consultants who have lived through an implementation, SI partners have no earned confidence to recommend your software. The solution is to manufacture the first 1–3 implementations by actively co-selling into existing enterprise opportunities in your direct pipeline where an SI already has a relationship. Offer to involve the SI as a "delivery partner" even on a deal you would have otherwise closed direct — accept the lower co-sell margin in exchange for the SI team gaining their first implementation experience. After 2–3 successful implementations, SI consultants start proactively recommending you to their other clients without any prompting.

**Problem 2: Different SI practice offices and geographies won't activate even when headquarters has signed the partnership.**
Root cause: Large GSIs (Deloitte, Accenture, EY) are organizational federations — the New York Finance Transformation practice and the Chicago Healthcare practice are essentially separate businesses with separate P&Ls and separate priorities. A national partner agreement means nothing to a practice director in a regional office who has never heard of your company. The solution is to map and personally engage at the practice level, not just the firm level. Identify the 3–4 practice areas within each GSI where your software is most relevant, find the practice director for each, and run a mini-version of the business case and certification program specifically for that practice. Build a "grassroots" network of individual SI practice advocates before trying to get firm-level commitment.

**Problem 3: SI partners claim credit for client relationships but won't actually engage on deals when you need them.**
Root cause: SI partners will accept MDF, attend executive dinners, and name-drop your software in proposals — but go quiet when you need them to make an introduction to a specific client executive for a stuck deal. This usually means your relationship is with a "partner development" or "alliance" contact at the SI (whose job is managing the paperwork) rather than the practice leaders who actually work with clients. The fix: shift all meaningful relationship activity to practice-level contacts (Managing Directors, Practice Directors, Senior Managers who do client work) and use the alliance contact only for administrative coordination. When a SI relationship is stuck on a deal, call the practice director directly and ask: "What would need to be true about this opportunity for you to feel comfortable making the introduction?" — this surfaces the real reason for inaction and gives you something specific to fix.
