## Overview
Designs and operationalizes a full co-marketing campaign system with technology partners — transforming passive integrations into active joint demand generation engines that produce partner-influenced and partner-sourced pipeline within 60 days. Use this when you have established technology partnerships but lack a repeatable campaign framework for generating revenue through them, when your ELG motion identifies target accounts but needs co-branded content and campaigns to convert them, or when you want to scale co-marketing beyond ad hoc joint webinars into a systematic, revenue-attributed program.

**CO-MARKETING PROGRAM ARCHITECTURE — FULL BUILD:**

**Section 1: Partnership Foundation & Co-Marketing Readiness Assessment**

Before designing campaigns, evaluate co-marketing readiness across five dimensions:

Dimension 1 — Audience Quality Alignment (score 1–5):
Does the partner's audience actually contain our ICP? A partner with 50,000 email subscribers who are all individual consultants delivers less value than a partner with 8,000 subscribers who are all VP-level at mid-market SaaS. Assessment method: request a sample audience breakdown from the partner (job titles, company sizes, industries of their email list or event attendees). Define the overlap % that makes co-marketing viable: our standard minimum is 25% true ICP overlap in the partner's accessible audience.

Dimension 2 — Content Production Capacity Parity (score 1–5):
Co-marketing fails when one company produces 80% of the content and the other shows up to put their logo on it. Assess: does the partner have a dedicated marketing resource who will commit to specific deliverables on specific dates? If not, design the program so that you produce the content and the partner distributes it — rather than expecting equal production. Define who produces what based on actual capacity, not aspirational commitment.

Dimension 3 — Brand and Messaging Compatibility (score 1–5):
Can both companies be positioned as equals or complementary in joint content without either company appearing subordinate? Brand mismatches — one company is enterprise-grade and the other is SMB-positioned, or one is privacy-first and the other monetizes data — create messaging conflicts that poison co-branded content. Identify any brand tensions upfront and define the "neutral ground" messaging territory where both companies' positioning is compatible.

Dimension 4 — Sales Alignment & Lead Routing Agreement (score 1–5):
What happens to leads generated from joint campaigns? Without a pre-agreed lead sharing protocol, co-marketing creates attribution disputes, duplicate outreach, and partner relationship damage. Define before launch: who owns leads from each campaign type, how lead data is shared (which fields, what format, what timing), whether both companies can market to all attendees or only their respective registrants, and how you will prevent partner reps from cold-calling leads the partner provided without consent.

Dimension 5 — Executive Sponsorship (score 1–5):
Is there executive awareness and support on both sides? Co-marketing programs stall when they exist purely at the practitioner level. Identify the executive sponsor at the partner company (ideally VP Marketing or CMO) and agree on a quarterly business review cadence to report co-marketing results upward. This is the accountability mechanism that prevents the program from dying when your contact at the partner changes jobs.

**Scoring interpretation:** 20–25 = High-readiness partner, pursue full co-marketing program immediately / 13–19 = Moderate readiness, start with a single joint webinar to test the partnership before committing to full program / 7–12 = Low readiness, invest in partner relationship development before co-marketing investment / 1–6 = Not ready for co-marketing — focus on integration adoption and mutual customer success first.

**Section 2: Joint Value Narrative Architecture**

The "better together" message is the foundation of all co-marketing. It must answer the question: why does a customer who uses BOTH products get an outcome they cannot achieve with either product alone?

Step 1 — Identify the Integration Use Case That Creates Unique Value:
List the top 3 customer workflows that the integration enables. For each, describe: the customer's before state (manual process, data silo, or outcome they could not achieve), the after state (what the integration makes possible), and the quantifiable outcome customers typically report (time saved, revenue generated, cost reduced, error rate eliminated). Choose the use case with the strongest outcome data as your primary campaign angle.

Step 2 — Write the Joint Value Proposition:
Use this formula: "[Our Product] + [Partner Product] = [specific outcome] for [specific persona] who [specific situation]."

Strong example: "Gong + Salesforce = Revenue intelligence that closes pipeline gaps automatically — for Revenue Operations Managers at B2B SaaS companies who spend more time reconciling forecast data than coaching their teams."

Weak example: "Together, our products help sales teams work better." — too vague, no persona, no quantified outcome.

Step 3 — Build the Proof Architecture:
Assemble the evidence that makes the joint value proposition believable. Minimum viable proof set: one mutual customer case study with at least one specific quantified outcome (never use a case study that says only "increased efficiency"), one benchmark statistic that frames the problem (e.g., "73% of RevOps managers report their CRM data is less than 80% accurate within 30 days of a rep entering it"), and one ROI calculation or savings estimate that the integration delivers. If no mutual customer case study exists yet, recruit one before launching any co-marketing campaigns — this is the non-negotiable foundation.

Step 4 — Define the Joint Content Tone and Brand Guidelines:
Write a one-page co-marketing style guide that both teams agree to: shared fonts and color treatment for co-branded assets, logo placement rules (never subordinate either company's logo — equal sizing and placement), voice guidelines (both companies tend toward [tone descriptor], avoid [tone or claims that conflict with either brand]), and approval process (maximum 48-hour turnaround for asset approvals from each company's marketing or legal team).

**Section 3: 90-Day Co-Marketing Campaign Calendar**

Structure the program in three 30-day phases, each building on the previous:

**Phase 1 (Days 1–30): Foundation and Audience Warm-Up**

Goal: Build joint awareness in both companies' audiences before asking for conversion.

Asset 1 — Partnership Announcement Blog Posts (produce two: one for your blog, one for theirs):
Your post angle: "How [Your Product] + [Partner Product] Solves [Joint ICP's Biggest Problem]" — 1,200–1,500 words, featuring the joint value narrative, integration use case walkthrough, and a mutual customer quote. Their post angle: same story, told from their product's perspective and optimized for their audience's search terms. Both posts live on separate domains but go live on the same day. Include CTAs linking to each other's integration documentation and a joint landing page for the webinar in Phase 2.

Asset 2 — Co-Announcement Email to Both Lists:
Each company sends a dedicated email to their own list introducing the partnership and the "better together" value. These are NOT identical — each is written in the sending company's voice and framed around what the partnership means for their own customers. Subject line formulas: [Your send] — "[Partner Name] + [Your Product]: Now you can [specific outcome]" / [Partner send] — "We've partnered with [Your Name] to solve [problem] — here's what that means for you." Include: partnership context (1 paragraph), 3 bullet points of specific integration benefits for this audience, a CTA to a joint resource page or webinar registration page, and a secondary CTA to the integration documentation.

Asset 3 — LinkedIn Social Content Series (5 posts each, published in the same week):
Format: LinkedIn carousels (6–8 slides) or text posts with a data-driven hook. Topics that perform: "[Number] things you didn't know [Product A] + [Product B] can do together" / "We surveyed [X] [joint ICP persona]s — here's what they said about [problem the integration solves]" / "The before/after: what [ICP workflow] looks like without vs. with [integration]." Both companies publish their version on the same day, and each company's social team engages (likes, comments) on the other's posts to amplify reach. This cross-company engagement signals to the LinkedIn algorithm that the content is worth distributing.

**Phase 2 (Days 31–60): Joint Lead Generation Event**

Goal: Convert joint audience awareness into measurable pipeline through a high-value lead generation event.

Asset 4 — Co-Branded Webinar (the centerpiece joint demand generation event):
Title formula: "[Outcome-Oriented Title]: How [Persona] Can [Achieve Specific Result] Using [Product A] + [Product B]" — example: "The RevOps Playbook: How to Close 40% More Pipeline by Connecting Your Sales Intelligence to Your CRM."
Format: 45 minutes of content + 15 minutes Q&A. Content structure: 5-minute joint intro (both presenters introduce each company and frame the integration use case), 15-minute "the problem" (data-driven case for why the current approach fails), 20-minute "the solution" (live demo or structured walkthrough of the integration in action — use a realistic customer scenario, not a product tour), 5-minute customer proof (brief case study or testimonial), and 10-minute Q&A.
Promotion: 4-email sequence over 3 weeks — Week 1 Day 1: save-the-date announcement to both lists, Week 2 Day 2: registration reminder with the session agenda and speaker bios, Week 3 Day 1: "One week away — here's what you'll learn" with a content teaser, Day of: morning reminder with the join link.
Registration page requirements: a single page hosted on whichever company has the stronger domain authority for the shared ICP's search terms, with both logos equally featured, the joint value headline, 4-bullet agenda, speaker headshots and bios, and a registration form that captures company name, job title, and one qualifying question relevant to pipeline routing ("How many sales reps does your team have?" or "Which challenge is most urgent for you?").

Asset 5 — Co-Authored Lead Magnet (primary gated content asset):
Format: 12–20 page definitive guide, original research report, or benchmark study on the topic the integration addresses. Title formula: "The [Year] State of [Topic] Report: How [Joint ICP Personas] Are [Addressing the Problem]" or "The [Persona]'s Guide to [Outcome]."
Gating strategy: host on your site if your SEO is stronger for the relevant keywords; gate with a form that passes leads to both companies (specify in lead sharing agreement). Include a co-branded cover page with both logos and a co-written foreword from a senior leader at each company.
Production process: one company drafts the full content, the other reviews and adds proprietary data, customer quotes, or research where available. Target production timeline: 4 weeks from kickoff to publication-ready PDF.
Promotion: promote in webinar follow-up sequence, LinkedIn organic series, paid syndication (see Section 5), and partner newsletter placements.

**Phase 3 (Days 61–90): Pipeline Conversion and Program Scale**

Goal: Convert engaged leads into sales opportunities and build the foundation for an ongoing co-marketing program.

Asset 6 — Mutual Customer Case Study:
Format: 800–1,000 word written case study + optional 2–3 minute customer video testimonial. Structure: customer context (company name, industry, size, their role and challenge), the evaluation and implementation (why they chose both products and how they set up the integration), the outcome (quantified results — specific metrics, not "improved efficiency"), and a direct customer quote. Distribution: both companies publish on their respective case study pages, link to each other's versions, and use in sales enablement and follow-up email sequences.

Asset 7 — Post-Webinar and Post-Download Nurture Sequences:
For webinar attendees who have not yet converted to a sales conversation: 3-email sequence over 14 days — Email 1 (day after event): webinar recording + link to lead magnet / Email 2 (day 7): one customer story or data point directly relevant to their qualifying question response from registration / Email 3 (day 14): direct CTA to book a demo or speak with a specialist, using a soft social proof frame ("Other [persona title]s who attended are asking us about [specific use case]"). Each company sends to their own registrants in their own email platform. Lead routing: registrants who click through to a demo CTA are routed to whichever company's sales team is best positioned to close based on the prospect's existing product usage.

**Section 4: Paid Co-Promotion Architecture**

LinkedIn Sponsored Content co-campaign:
Targeting: [Job Title Exact Match: VP Revenue Operations, Director Revenue Operations, Head of RevOps, VP Sales Operations] + [Company Size: 50–500 employees] + [Industry: Computer Software, Internet, Financial Services (as relevant)] + [Geography: US, Canada, UK, Australia].
Ad formats: Single image ads (primary), Document ads promoting the lead magnet download (high B2B conversion rate for content), and Thought Leader Ads using a post from a senior leader at each company (available if either company's executive has a LinkedIn following >5,000).
Creative brief for 3 ad variations: Variation A — social proof angle: "[X] revenue operations teams use [Product A] + [Product B] together — here's what they're doing differently"; Variation B — problem/insight angle: "Your CRM forecast is probably wrong. Here's why [integration] fixes it"; Variation C — outcome anchor: "Close [X]% more pipeline — [product pairing] makes it possible."
Budget split model: each company contributes equal spend for campaigns targeting the joint ICP; the company whose ICP is being targeted contributes a higher share for persona-specific campaigns targeting their own primary buyer.

Content syndication:
Syndicate the lead magnet report through 1–2 content syndication networks (Netline, TechTarget, IDG, or relevant vertical publisher) targeting the shared ICP job titles and company sizes. Budget: $3,000–$8,000 per campaign typically generates 150–400 co-branded downloads from net-new contacts. Attribute all syndication leads to the co-marketing campaign in both CRMs.

Newsletter sponsorships:
Identify 3–5 newsletters read by the shared ICP (often found by surveying your customers — "What newsletters do you read to stay current on [topic]?") and negotiate co-branded placement. Frame as a "tools we recommend together" editorial format rather than traditional display ads. Cost-per-read ranges from $0.50–$3.00 for niche B2B newsletters. Both companies share the cost and both companies' lead routing receives any leads generated.

**Section 5: Lead Sharing, Attribution, and Governance Model**

Lead sharing protocol (must be agreed in writing before any campaign launches):

Lead ownership rules: leads generated by each company's own distribution (their email list, their social audience, their paid spend) belong to that company unless the prospect explicitly engaged with the other company's content. Leads generated by joint campaigns (webinar registrations where both companies promoted, gated content hosted jointly) should be shared to both companies' CRMs.

Data transfer specification: agree on exact fields to be shared — minimum: first name, last name, email, company name, job title, opt-in consent status, and which specific campaign/asset they engaged with. Transfer method: automated via shared Zapier/Workato flow, HubSpot list export on agreed cadence, or Salesforce connected app. Frequency: within 48 hours of any lead conversion event.

Consent and compliance: ensure that registration forms for joint campaigns include clear language covering data sharing with both companies: "By registering, you agree to receive communications from [Company A] and [Company B]." Both companies must maintain this lead data in compliance with GDPR/CCPA as applicable.

No double-selling rule: sales teams at both companies must not independently cold-call the same prospect using the shared lead list. Define a "mutual account" flag in both CRMs for prospects in both databases, and agree that the partner whose product the prospect is currently evaluating gets the right-of-first-contact, with the other company on a 30-day delay.

**Section 6: Measurement Framework and Reporting Cadence**

7 KPIs that prove co-marketing program ROI:

1. Partner-influenced pipeline value ($): all pipeline opportunities where a prospect engaged with co-marketing content before entering or while progressing through the pipeline. Track by campaign source tags in CRM.
2. Partner-sourced MQL volume: new marketing-qualified leads originating exclusively from partner-driven channels (partner email list, partner social promotion, partner marketplace listing).
3. Joint content conversion rate: % of joint content consumers (webinar registrants, lead magnet downloaders) who convert to a sales-qualified opportunity within 90 days. Benchmark: 4–8% for B2B SaaS co-marketing.
4. Webinar show rate: % of registrants who attend live. Benchmark: 35–50% for co-hosted webinars (typically higher than single-company webinars because two companies' audiences are attending).
5. Cost-per-partner-MQL vs. solo-MQL: total co-marketing investment divided by partner-sourced MQLs, compared to your average solo-channel MQL cost. Target: co-marketing MQL cost should be 20–40% lower due to shared audience and shared spend.
6. Time-to-opportunity for partner-influenced leads: average days from first co-marketing engagement to opportunity creation. Benchmark: typically 15–25% faster than cold inbound because the partner relationship provides implicit trust.
7. Co-marketing program NPS (partner satisfaction score): quarterly survey of 3–5 questions sent to your partner marketing contact rating the program on: quality of leads generated, ease of content collaboration, communication quality, and overall program satisfaction. Minimum viable score: 7/10.

Reporting cadence: weekly internal metrics review (pipeline influenced, MQL volume, content performance), monthly joint report shared with partner contact (Google Slides or PowerPoint template agreed upfront — 5 slides: executive summary, campaign performance vs. targets, lead pipeline summary, highlights and challenges, next 30-day plan), quarterly executive review (present to CMO or VP Marketing at both companies — include ROI calculation, projected full-year impact, and investment request for next quarter).

## Integration Tips

- **HubSpot:** Create a dedicated co-marketing campaign folder in HubSpot Campaigns with campaign tags for each partner. Use HubSpot's contact source tracking to attribute leads to partner co-marketing emails vs. your own. Set up a shared contact property "Partner Co-Marketing Source" to flag all contacts acquired through joint campaigns for segmented reporting.
- **Salesforce:** Create a custom Campaign Type = "Partner Co-Marketing" and use Campaign Influence to track pipeline influence across contacts who engaged with co-marketing before opportunity creation. Build a Partner Co-Marketing dashboard in Salesforce Reports with the 7 KPIs defined in Section 6.
- **Google Sheets / Notion:** Use a shared content production tracker accessible by both companies — columns: asset type, working title, author, reviewer, due date, publish date, status (in progress / in review / live), target metric, and actual result. This replaces 90% of co-marketing status meetings.
- **Zapier / Workato:** Automate lead sharing between both companies' CRMs post-webinar registration — triggers: new contact added to a specific HubSpot list or Salesforce campaign → creates or updates contact record in partner's CRM with shared fields. Include a 24-hour delay to allow for manual review before automation fires.
- **LinkedIn Campaign Manager:** Both companies should add each other as "linked accounts" in LinkedIn Campaign Manager to enable cross-company audience matching and to share conversion tracking across both ad accounts for co-invest campaigns.
- **Loom:** Use Loom video messages for async communication between co-marketing teams across companies — screen recordings are faster than written briefs for approving ad creative, reviewing email copy, or aligning on campaign changes without scheduling a meeting.

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: The partner keeps delaying content approvals and the campaign timeline is slipping.**
Solution: Establish a written 48-hour approval SLA at program kickoff and include it in the co-marketing agreement. When assets are shared for review, send a Loom walkthrough of the asset simultaneously — partners are more likely to engage with a 90-second video than a written brief. If approvals routinely exceed 48 hours, escalate to the partner's marketing leadership and reframe: "Our joint webinar has 300 registrants waiting. We need approval by Thursday to send the promotional email or we risk poor show rate." Deadlines with downstream consequences move faster than open-ended review requests.

**Problem: The partner webinar generated great registrations but almost no sales pipeline for our team — all the SQLs went to the partner.**
Solution: This happens when the webinar topic is more aligned with the partner's product use case than yours. Fix this in future campaigns by designing the webinar content so that the "pain point" section is squarely in your product's solution territory, and the "implementation" section demonstrates your product as the execution layer.
