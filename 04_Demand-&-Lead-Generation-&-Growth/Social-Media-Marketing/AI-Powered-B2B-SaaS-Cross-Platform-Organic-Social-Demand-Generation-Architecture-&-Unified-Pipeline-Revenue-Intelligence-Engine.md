## Troubleshooting

**Problem: Content is performing well on LinkedIn but getting zero traction on YouTube and TikTok**
Solution: LinkedIn and short-form video are fundamentally different psychological environments. LinkedIn audiences reward expertise and professional insight; YouTube and TikTok audiences reward entertainment value and immediate practical payoff. The fix is NOT creating separate content strategies — it's adjusting the presentation layer. Take the same core insight from your top LinkedIn post and reframe the opening: LinkedIn version starts with "Here's what we learned from 10,000 sales calls." YouTube/TikTok version starts with "If you're still running weekly coaching sessions, you're already losing." The insight is the same; the hook energy is completely different. Run a 4-week test where every LinkedIn post is manually converted into a TikTok hook using this "emotional stakes first" principle before judging platform viability.

**Problem: Cross-platform effort is too high — the team is burning out trying to post on 4 platforms weekly**
Solution: This is a sequencing problem, not a capacity problem. Most B2B SaaS companies overestimate how many platforms they should activate simultaneously. The correct order: (1) Achieve LinkedIn consistency and early traction (90 days minimum), (2) Add YouTube with a focused 3-video/month cadence around specific ICP search terms, (3) Only add TikTok/Reels when a video content workflow already exists from YouTube production. Running a lean 2-platform strategy well outperforms a 4-platform strategy poorly executed. Use the Platform Role Matrix to identify your single highest-ROI platform and put 80% of effort there for the first 90 days. Add the second platform only when the first is generating measurable pipeline.

**Problem: Can't attribute pipeline to organic social — finance team says "correlation is not causation"**
Solution: Run the "organic social pause test" — stop all organic social posting for 30 days across all platforms, then measure the change in (a) self-reported attribution responses on demo forms, (b) direct traffic from ICP company domains, and (c) inbound demo requests mentioning specific content. If organic social is driving demand, all three metrics will drop noticeably. This is the closest thing to a controlled experiment you can run without a true A/B test. If the drop is visible, you have causal evidence. If it's not visible, you either weren't generating meaningful demand or your attribution measurement is incomplete. Either way, the test gives you actionable data. Present results to finance as: "We ran a 30-day organic social silence test. Here's what happened to our inbound pipeline during that window versus the prior 30 days."
