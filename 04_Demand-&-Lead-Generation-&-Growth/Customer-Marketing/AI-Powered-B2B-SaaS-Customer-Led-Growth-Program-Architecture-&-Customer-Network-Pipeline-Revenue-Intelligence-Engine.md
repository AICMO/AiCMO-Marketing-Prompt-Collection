### CLG Program Architecture

#### Section 1: The Five CLG Motions

A Customer-Led Growth program is not a single referral program. It is a portfolio of five distinct motions, each generating pipeline through a different mechanism. Design all five — then weight investment by your company's current stage and customer base maturity.

**Motion 1: Champion Warm Introduction Program (Highest Intent, Highest Conversion)**

*What it is*: A structured process by which customer champions proactively introduce your company to a specific prospect in their network — a peer they know, trust, and can vouch for. This is not a referral link. It is a human-mediated warm handoff.

*Why it converts*: Warm introductions from trusted peers convert to opportunity at 30-50% — 5-10x the rate of cold outbound. A prospect who hears "we use this and it changed how our team works" from a peer they respect enters the conversation already trusting the product.

*Program design*:

**Champion identification** — Build a Champion Scorecard. Score each customer contact (not account) on:
- NPS score (submitted) or sentiment score (derived from CS interaction notes via AI sentiment analysis): 0-25 pts
- Product engagement depth (power user status — daily active, uses advanced features, has invited colleagues): 0-20 pts
- Tenure with product (6+ months = higher credibility as an introducer): 0-15 pts
- Seniority and network quality (Director+ with active LinkedIn presence, conference speaker, known in their community): 0-20 pts
- Prior advocacy acts (participated in case study, reference call, webinar): 0-20 pts

Score threshold for program invitation: ≥ 65/100. Export from CRM monthly. CS validates list before any outreach. Expected eligible population: 3-8% of customer contacts.

**Champion activation sequence**:
- Step 1 (CS-led): CSM mentions the program organically in a QBR or check-in call: "We're building a small group of customers who are willing to share their experience with peers in their network — would you be open to an intro conversation with our marketing team?"
- Step 2 (Marketing-led intro call, 20 min): Customer Marketing Manager meets the champion. Goal: understand their network, how they talk about the product, and what types of companies they interact with. No ask for introductions yet. Build the relationship.
- Step 3 (Network mapping exercise): After the intro call, send a 3-question async survey (Typeform, 5 min): "Can you name 2-3 peers at other companies who face the same challenges you were solving when you started using [Product]?" + "Would you be comfortable making a personal introduction — not a sales referral, just a 'you should talk to these people' message?" + "What's the best format for you — email intro, LinkedIn DM, coffee at a conference?"
- Step 4 (Introduction facilitation): When a champion names a prospect, customer marketing researches the prospect in the CRM/database (do they exist as a target? are they an ICP fit?). If yes, draft a personalized introduction message FOR the champion — they review, edit, and send in their own voice. Marketing does the work; the champion adds the credibility.
- Step 5 (Warm handoff to sales): Once the introduction is made and the prospect responds, sales receives a "Champion Warm Intro" lead with: champion name, relationship context, what the champion said, and the prospect's initial response. Sales opens with full context and zero cold outreach mechanics.

**Incentive design**:
- Philosophical rule: Incentivize participation, never introductions. The moment a champion feels they're "selling for money" they stop being a credible peer reference. Never pay per referral.
- Incentives that work: exclusive event invitations, early product access, featured thought leadership content (ghostwritten LinkedIn posts amplified by your brand), donation to their charity of choice in their name, recognition in your customer community.
- Cadence: Champions receive quarterly check-ins from customer marketing (not CS). Small recognition moments: "We wanted you to know that the introduction you made to [Prospect] turned into a conversation — thank you."

**Expected output**: A program with 25-75 active champions generates 8-20 qualified warm introductions per quarter. At 30-40% opportunity conversion, that is 3-8 new opportunities per quarter, or $X pipeline at your ACV.

**Motion 2: Customer Co-Marketing Campaigns (Brand Authority × Customer Network Reach)**

*What it is*: Joint marketing initiatives where your company and a customer brand co-create content, co-host events, or co-author research — and both parties promote to their respective audiences. Your brand gets access to the customer's audience; the customer gets thought leadership positioning and co-marketing budget.

*Why it converts*: A prospect who sees a company they already admire co-authoring content with your brand transfers that trust to your company. Co-marketing also unlocks audiences you don't have organic access to — a Fortune 500 customer co-hosting a webinar with you reaches their network, which is far harder (and more expensive) to reach via paid media alone.

*Program design*:

**Co-marketing asset tiers:**

| Tier | Format | Customer Ask | Marketing Investment | Expected Reach |
|------|--------|-------------|---------------------|----------------|
| Tier 1 (Lowest ask) | Customer quote in blog post or press release | 20 min review + approval | $0 | 1,000-5,000 impressions |
| Tier 2 | Co-authored article (bylined by both companies) | 1-2 hours of input, marketing ghosts | $1,000-2,000 content production | 5,000-20,000 impressions |
| Tier 3 | Joint webinar or podcast episode | 60-90 min live participation | $2,000-5,000 event production | 200-800 registrants |
| Tier 4 | Co-authored original research report | 3-5 hours of review + customer data contribution | $10,000-25,000 research + design | 2,000-10,000 downloads, media coverage |
| Tier 5 | Co-sponsored conference session or keynote | Significant time investment, executive availability | $15,000-50,000 event sponsorship | 500-2,000 live attendees |

**Co-marketing selection criteria**: Only invite customers to co-market when:
- Their brand name is recognized by your ICP (name-drop effect in promotion)
- Their audience has meaningful overlap with your ICP (otherwise you're building their brand without a pipeline return)
- The customer contact has genuine expertise to contribute — co-marketing that feels like a customer testimonial in disguise alienates practitioners

**Content that works specifically for co-marketing**:
- "How [Customer Company] Solved [Universal ICP Problem]: A Practitioner's Playbook" — the customer's story, positioned as universal best practice
- "[Trend/Topic]: What [X] Practitioners Are Actually Doing" — original research using aggregated (anonymized) customer data + named contributors from willing customers
- "[Problem] Masterclass" webinar — customer practitioner teaches the methodology; your product is the tooling they use (demonstrated naturally, not pitched)

**Promotion split**: Customer shares to their LinkedIn network, their newsletter (if they have one), and their internal champions share to personal networks. Marketing drives paid promotion to lookalike audiences and handles all registration/follow-up infrastructure.

**Attribution**: All co-marketing registrations and content downloads are captured in MAP. Any contact from a co-marketing asset who converts to an opportunity within 90 days is tagged "Customer Co-Marketing — [Customer Name]" in the CRM opportunity record.

**Motion 3: Customer-Powered Event Strategy (Peer Trust at Scale)**

*What it is*: Hosted events (dinners, roundtables, user group meetups, exclusive summits) where your best customers are present as peers, not salespeople — and prospects are invited into the room specifically to learn from those customers. The product is discussed naturally, not pitched. The relationship between your brand and the customer community is what closes deals.

*Why it converts*: A prospect at a dinner where 3 customers are openly discussing how they use your product in a peer conversation converts at dramatically higher rates than a prospect who attended a product webinar. The buying psychology is entirely different: they are not being sold to, they are learning from people like them.

*Event program design*:

**Event type 1: Executive Roundtable (6-12 attendees, 50/50 customer/prospect)**
- Format: 90-minute facilitated dinner or lunch; no presentations, no demos. Discussion structured around a business challenge (e.g., "How are CISOs managing AI security risk in 2026?"). 3-4 customer executives in the room who naturally reference how they've addressed the challenge using [Product].
- Prospect invitation: Sales nominates target accounts; customer marketing confirms prospect fit and invites via personal note from a named customer executive in the room: "I'm joining a small dinner on [date] with [other executives] to discuss [topic] — would love to have your perspective in the room." Prospects convert invitations at 2-3x the rate of vendor-only dinners.
- Post-event sequence: Marketing sends personalized follow-up within 24 hours: "Thank you for joining the conversation last night. [Customer Name] mentioned they'd be happy to share a bit more about how they approached [specific topic] — want me to set that up?" → Low-pressure warm intro to a customer reference.
- Expected conversion: 25-40% of prospect attendees convert to qualified opportunity within 90 days.

**Event type 2: Customer User Group (Annual or Semi-Annual, 50-200 attendees, 60-70% customers, 30-40% invited prospects)**
- Format: Half-day or full-day practitioner event. Agenda is 80% customer speaker presentations, 20% product roadmap. No "sales zone" in the agenda. Prospects attend because they want to learn from the practitioner content.
- Prospect invitation: Frame as an "invitation to learn from practitioners" not as a vendor event. Let customer speakers be the draw.
- Co-location strategy: Host user groups the day before or after major industry conferences — attendees are already traveling, the marginal cost of adding a day is low.

**Event type 3: Digital Peer Roundtable (Virtual, 8-12 attendees, quarterly)**
- Format: 60-minute Zoom roundtable facilitated by customer marketing. 3-4 customer speakers share 10-minute practitioner case studies. 4-6 invited prospects as "peer participants." No screen-share demos. No slides beyond the customers' own content.
- Scale advantage: Digital eliminates travel; can run quarterly or monthly at low incremental cost. Different customer speakers each time = recurring programming.
- Expected output: 8-12 digital roundtables per year × 4-6 prospects per event = 48-72 net-new prospect touchpoints per year at a cost dramatically lower than field events.

**Motion 4: Customer Proof Interception at Buyer Decision Points (Right Place, Right Time)**

*What it is*: The systematic deployment of customer proof — case studies, video testimonials, G2 reviews, peer quotes — at the exact digital locations where your ICP is actively evaluating solutions. Not just publishing proof content and hoping buyers find it, but engineering its placement into the buying journey.

*Why it converts*: 72% of B2B buyers say peer reviews and case studies are the most influential content in their final vendor decision. The window where proof converts is narrow — active evaluation, not awareness. Deploying proof at the top of funnel wastes credibility; deploying at the moment of evaluation maximizes it.

*Proof interception playbook*:

**Interception Point 1: Mid-Funnel Web Behavior (Pricing/Demo Pages)**
- Trigger: Account-level web analytics (6sense/Demandbase) shows a target account contact visiting the pricing page, demo request page, or comparison pages ≥ 2 times in 7 days.
- Automated response: MAP enrolls the identified contact in a "Peer Proof Nurture" — a 3-email sequence featuring: (1) Customer story from their industry with specific ROI metric, (2) "What customers say before vs. after" — 3 verbatim quotes from similar companies, (3) "Would a peer perspective help?" — offer a live customer reference call, not a sales demo.
- Logic: Mid-funnel visitors are already interested; they need social validation, not more product information. Proof at this stage is more persuasive than any feature email.

**Interception Point 2: Competitor Comparison Pages**
- Where: Your own competitor comparison pages (e.g., "[Product] vs. [Competitor]") AND third-party review sites (G2, Capterra, TrustRadius).
- Tactic A (owned pages): Feature 3-5 customer quotes specifically addressing why they chose you over that competitor. Run quarterly interviews with customers who switched from each major competitor and extract verbatim displacement language. Quote: "[Competitor] couldn't do X — [Product] did X out of the box. That was the deciding factor." — this is 10x more credible than any company marketing copy.
- Tactic B (G2/Capterra): Run a systematic G2 review generation campaign targeting customers with high NPS (≥ 8) in segments where review volume is thin. G2 filters allow buyers to sort by company size, industry, and use case — if your reviews are concentrated in one segment, buyers in other segments won't find relevant proof.
- Automated G2 campaign: NPS survey response ≥ 8 → 3 days later → automated email (from Customer Marketing Manager): "Your experience with [Product] matters to peers who are evaluating the same decision you made X months ago. Would you take 5 minutes to share your experience on G2? [Direct link to G2 review form]." Completion rate for this sequence: 15-30% when targeted at promoters.

**Interception Point 3: Sales Opportunity Stages (Deal Acceleration)**
- Trigger: Salesforce opportunity moves to Stage 3 (Evaluation) or later.
- Automated alert to AE: CRM workflow sends a Slack/email to the assigned AE with: "[Deal Name] just moved to Evaluation. Here are the 3 most relevant customer proof points for a [Industry, Size, Use Case] buyer: [Link to Case Study 1, Video 2, Quote 3]." No manual search required — AI/CRM automation surfaces the right proof for each deal's specific context.
- One-click reference matching: AE clicks a link in Salesforce that opens a ReferenceEdge/Orca-style interface showing: "Customers willing to speak as references for this deal's profile ([Industry, Company Size, Use Case])." AE requests reference; platform notifies CS; CS confirms availability within 24 hours. Reference call scheduled with zero manual coordination.

**Interception Point 4: Outbound Prospecting Personalization**
- Tactic: Build a "proof snippet library" — 50-100 pre-approved, verbatim customer quotes organized by: industry, company size, persona (buyer role), use case, and competitor displacement. SDRs/AEs pull the most relevant snippet into cold outreach as the opening credibility hook.
- Example: "VP of RevOps at [Peer Company] said '[verbatim quote about time-to-value in the first 90 days]' — given you're running the same function at [Prospect Company], I thought it might resonate."
- Library maintenance: Customer marketing refreshes quarterly. New case study → extract 5 proof snippets and add to library with metadata tags. Average active library: 50-100 snippets covering top 5 industries and top 3 buyer personas.

**Motion 5: Customer Community as Demand Engine (Long-Term, Compounding)**

*What it is*: A customer community (Slack, Circle, private LinkedIn group, or in-person network) that becomes a place where prospects want to participate — because the conversation and peer learning is so valuable that being a community member is itself a reason to evaluate your product. The community drives demand by making prospects want to be part of what your customers have built together.

*Why it converts*: The most defensible demand generation channel is one your competitors cannot replicate or outspend. A thriving customer community with 500+ engaged practitioners is worth more than any paid media budget, because the trust and peer-to-peer dialogue it generates is not buyable.

*Community-as-demand-gen design*:

**Prospect participation strategy**: Invite high-ICP prospects (based on intent signals + SDR target lists) to join the community as "guest members" or invite them to a community event. Frame it as: "This community is primarily for customers, but we occasionally open it to practitioners who are evaluating the space — the conversation is practitioner-led, not vendor-led."

**Content strategy for demand**: The community calendar should include 2-4 events/month that are open (or partially open) to non-customers: AMAs with customer practitioners, peer benchmarking exercises, group problem-solving sessions. These create observable value for prospects who haven't purchased yet.

**Community → pipeline workflow**: Any non-customer who participates in a community event is captured in MAP with the activity logged. Community engagement score (events attended, posts made, reactions given) feeds into intent scoring. When a non-customer's community engagement score exceeds threshold: sales receives an alert — "[Prospect Name] at [Company] has attended 3 community events and posted twice in the last 30 days. High engagement, no active opportunity." Sales outreach opens with: "I noticed you've been participating in the [Community Name] community — I'd love to get your perspective on what's been most valuable."

#### Section 2: Champion Network Operating Model

Running a champion program at scale requires process, not just relationships. Define the operating model:

**Champion Roster Management (CRM or advocacy platform):**
Build a Champion Object in Salesforce (or use Influitive/Orca/ReferenceEdge):
- Champion Tier: A (actively making introductions), B (willing to participate in co-marketing), C (available for reference calls only)
- Last Customer Marketing Contact Date
- Network Mapping Status: Completed / Pending / Not Started
- Introductions Made: [#] total, [#] converted to opportunity
- Co-Marketing Participations: [#]
- Reference Calls Completed: [#]
- Champion Health: Active / Dormant (no engagement in 90 days) / At Risk (customer health amber/red)

**Champion Lifecycle:**
1. **Identification** (monthly): CS + product analytics data → Champion Scorecard → CS validation → Customer Marketing adds to "Champion Pipeline" list
2. **Activation** (weeks 1-4): Customer Marketing intro call → network mapping survey → first co-marketing or reference ask
3. **Nurture** (ongoing): Quarterly touchpoint from Customer Marketing (separate from CS cadence). Annual "Champion Summit" or exclusive experience. Continuous recognition.
4. **Reactivation** (when dormant): After 90 days of no engagement, Customer Marketing sends personal note (not automated): "It's been a while — how are things going with [Product]? I'd love to reconnect." If no response after 2 attempts: move to C-tier (reference only, no introduction asks).
5. **Offboarding** (when necessary): If customer health goes red or contact leaves the company: immediately pause all champion activities. CS notified. If contact leaves for another ICP-fit company: update champion record to new company — they may become a new champion at their next employer (common in SaaS).

#### Section 3: CLG Attribution and Revenue Measurement

Track customer-sourced pipeline as a distinct category:

**CRM Configuration:**
- Opportunity field: "Pipeline Source" = Net New / Expansion / Customer-Sourced (sub-categories: Warm Introduction / Co-Marketing / Community / Proof Intercept / Event)
- Lead/Contact field: "CLG Program Participation" = Champion Network / Co-Marketing Partner / Community Member / Event Attendee

**Monthly CLG Scorecard:**

| Metric | Definition | Target |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| Active Champions (Tier A+B) | Champions who engaged with Marketing in last 90 days | 25-75 |
| Warm Introductions Made | Total this quarter | 8-20 |
| Introduction-to-Opportunity Rate | % of warm intros that became qualified opportunities | 30-45% |
| Customer-Sourced New Logo Pipeline | $ value of net-new opps where CLG was source | $X |
| Customer-Sourced New Logo Closed Won | $ ARR closed from CLG-sourced opps | $X |
| Co-Marketing Leads Generated | New contacts acquired through co-marketing | X/quarter |
| Event Prospect Attendees | Non-customers who attended CLG-hosted events | X/quarter |
| CLG Program ROI | CLG-sourced closed ARR ÷ CLG program investment | ≥ 8:1 |

**Quarterly Board Metric:**
"Customer-Sourced Pipeline" = All opportunities created in the quarter where the initial contact, lead source, or influencing campaign is tagged to the CLG program. Report as a % of total new logo pipeline — target is 15-25% of new logo pipeline sourced from CLG motions by month 12 of the program.

## Success Metrics

A high-quality CLG program design from this prompt will:
- Identify at least 20 specific champion candidates by profile (not just "find enthusiastic customers") with a scoring model that can be applied to the CRM
- Design co-marketing initiatives where the customer's brand creates genuine audience reach — not just asking customers to share vendor content
- Produce event formats where the prospect feels they are being invited into a peer community, not a sales dinner
- Define proof interception at 3+ specific buyer journey moments with automated trigger logic (not "publish case studies on your website")
- Calculate expected pipeline contribution: [# champions] × [introductions per champion per quarter] × [introduction-to-opportunity rate] × [opportunity ACV] = projected CLG pipeline

**Program maturity benchmarks:**
- Month 3: First 15-25 champions activated; first co-marketing asset published; first roundtable event held; first CLG attribution report
- Month 6: 3-5 warm introduction opportunities in pipeline; CLG contributing 5-8% of new logo pipeline
- Month 12: CLG contributing 15-25% of new logo pipeline; customer-sourced CAC at least 3x lower than paid media CAC; program ROI ≥ 8:1

## Troubleshooting

**Problem:** Champions say they are willing to make introductions, but when you send them the draft introduction message, they delay, edit extensively, or never send it — program stalls despite willing participants.
**Fix:** The introduction message is doing too much work. Champions hesitate when the draft reads like a sales email — they don't want to put their credibility on the line for a vendor pitch. Rewrite every introduction draft to be: (1) extremely short — 3-4 sentences max, (2) peer-to-peer in tone, not promotional, (3) focused entirely on the prospect's potential interest, not the product's features. Example reframe: Instead of "I'd like to introduce you to [Company] — they help RevOps teams with revenue intelligence, forecasting, and pipeline tracking," write: "I've been talking with the team at [Company] about pipeline accuracy challenges — sounds like you've been dealing with similar things at [Prospect Company]. Thought it might be worth a 20-minute conversation." The champion then sends in their own words with minimal editing. Follow-up: have your CEO or VP of Marketing send champions a personal thank-you video within 24 hours of any introduction sent — this recognition dramatically increases repeat participation.

**Problem:** Co-marketing webinars consistently attract high registration but low attendance (show rate under 20%), making the investment feel unjustifiable even when the content is strong.
**Fix:** Low show rates in co-marketing webinars are almost always a registration-to-attendance communication problem, not an audience or content problem. Implement a 5-touch attendance confirmation sequence: (1) Confirmation email immediately post-registration with a calendar invite attachment (not just a link), (2) Text/SMS reminder 48 hours before from the customer speaker — "Looking forward to sharing [specific thing] with you on [date]" — this personal touch is the highest-lift attendance driver, (3) Email reminder day-before from Customer Marketing Manager with a preview of what the customer will share, (4) 15-minute-before reminder from MAP with direct join link (not the registration confirmation link — a direct URL reduces friction), (5) Post-event recording email sent within 4 hours for no-shows. With this sequence, show rates typically climb from 15-20% to 35-50% without changing the content or audience.

**Problem:** The CLG program is generating warm introductions and event-sourced leads, but Sales is treating them identically to cold leads — not following up faster, not opening with the peer context, and attribution is getting lost because AEs are creating opportunities under "Outbound" source.
**Fix:** This is a sales enablement and process accountability gap, not a marketing problem. Run a 45-minute enablement session with the sales team showing the performance data contrast: warm introduction opportunities close 40% faster and at 15% higher ACV than cold outbound in most SaaS companies (use your own data if you have it, or industry benchmarks if you don't). Make CLG source handling a specific behavior in sales playbooks: every warm intro opportunity must open with explicit reference to the customer relationship ("[Champion Name] mentioned you might be facing [specific challenge] — that's exactly what they came to us to solve 14 months ago"). For CRM hygiene: work with Revenue Operations to make "CLG — [specific motion]" a required picklist value in "Opportunity Source" and build a validation rule that prevents stage advancement without source populated. Non-negotiable data entry is the only long-term fix for attribution loss.
