## Overview

NARRATIVE ARCHITECTURE:

Apply the B2B Creator Narrative Framework — five structural layers:

Layer 1 — SHARED PAIN ACKNOWLEDGMENT
Open with the pain the creator's audience already feels — not the product. The creator must earn the right to mention the sponsor by establishing they understand the reader's reality.
- Pain statement: [Derived from ICP pain above — must sound like something the audience would say, not something marketing would write]
- Credibility anchor: [Creator's personal experience with this pain — ask creator to provide one sentence from their own experience]
- Stakes statement: [What happens to the audience if this pain goes unsolved — business consequence, career consequence, or emotional consequence]

Layer 2 — PATTERN INTERRUPT / INSIGHT
The creator introduces a counterintuitive insight or reframes how the audience thinks about the problem. This is where the creator's original thinking comes in — not the product.
- Reframe: [e.g., "The forecast problem isn't a rep behavior problem — it's a data architecture problem. You can't coach your way out of bad inputs."]
- Supporting evidence: [Stat, personal experience, or observable trend that validates the reframe — must come from a credible, citable source]

Layer 3 — SOLUTION BRIDGE

- Creator's genuine assessment hook: [e.g., "I was skeptical of another 'AI forecasting' tool — until I saw what it actually does with activity data instead of CRM inputs"]
- Differentiator in practitioner language: [Translate the feature into a practitioner outcome — avoid product jargon]

PLATFORM-SPECIFIC FORMAT GUIDANCE:

LinkedIn Long-Form Post (600–900 words):

- Slide 6: Key proof point — single stat or case study
- Slide 7: CTA slide — offer + link + disclosure
- Slide 8 (optional): "Save for later" prompt with key takeaway summary
- Design brief: [Brand colors/style if providing templates, or guidance for creator's own design]

YouTube Sponsored Segment (60–90 second mid-roll):
- Transition line: Creator-native phrase to transition from content ("which brings me to today's sponsor")

Podcast Ad Read (30-second or 60-second):

CONTENT REVIEW FRAMEWORK:

Before approving any creator draft, score it against these criteria (flag for revision if any criterion fails):

1. Credibility check: Are all statistics accurate and citable? Any claim that can't be verified must be removed.
2. Brand voice alignment: Does the creator's framing contradict or undermine the brand's positioning?
3. Audience relevance: Does the opening hook speak to this creator's specific audience's pain — or does it feel like a generic ad?
4. Conversion path integrity: Is the CTA clear, low-friction, and linked correctly? Does UTM parameter match the pre-agreed tracking string?
5. FTC compliance: Is sponsorship disclosed clearly, early enough, and in a way that's consistent with platform rules?
6. Forbidden messaging audit: Does the draft contain any prohibited claims, competitor references, or unsubstantiated ROI guarantees?
7. Creator voice preservation: Does this sound like the creator or like corporate copy? (If marketing team could have written it without the creator, it will underperform.)
8. Engagement bait assessment: Does the CTA or framing encourage authentic audience dialogue — or does it feel like it's optimizing for likes rather than conversions?

REVISION FEEDBACK PROTOCOL:
When giving creators feedback on drafts:
- Lead with what's working (be specific — quote the line)
- Flag the exact issue with a specific suggested alternative (not "make it less salesy" — show them what less salesy looks like)
- Limit revision asks to 3 maximum — more than 3 changes signals the brief was unclear, not that the creator failed
- Give a 72-hour turnaround commitment from feedback submission to revised draft

## Success Metrics

- **Creator revision cycles**: Target ≤ 1.2 rounds of revision per content piece (well-briefed creators need minimal back-and-forth)
- **Content approval rate**: ≥ 85% of creator drafts approved without substantive changes
- **Engagement quality**: Sponsored content achieves ≥ 60% of creator's average organic engagement rate (below 40% = brief failure)
- **UTM conversion rate**: Track link clicks → trial/demo starts; benchmark: 2–5% click-to-conversion for qualified audiences
- **Comment sentiment**: Sample first 20 comments per post; target ≤ 15% negative/skeptical comments about the sponsorship
- **Creator satisfaction score**: After campaign, ask creator on a 1–5 scale how clear and useful the brief was; target ≥ 4.2/5
- **Brand message accuracy**: ≥ 4 of 5 core talking points accurately represented in final published content

## Integration Tips

- **Notion**: Build a Creator Brief Template database with fields for each campaign parameter — duplicate per creator per campaign; share the "Creator-Facing" view (strip internal notes) directly with creators as a linked page
- **Google Docs**: Use commenting for revision feedback — tag creators with @mention and label comments as "Must Fix" vs. "Suggestion" to prioritize their attention

- **Riverside / Descript**: For podcast creators, record a 10-minute brand briefing session where you verbally explain the narrative arc — transcribe and share as an audio reference alongside the written brief
- **Canva**: Provide carousel creators with a branded Canva template pre-populated with your color palette and font — reduces design friction and ensures brand consistency without restricting creative expression

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: Creator's published content sounds like an ad, not their voice — audience reacts negatively in comments**
Solution: Audit the brief for corporate language that leaked into the talking points. Common offenders: phrases like "industry-leading," "best-in-class," "powerful platform," and any ROI claim presented as fact rather than customer experience. Rewrite every talking point to pass the "would a peer say this to a peer" test. In the next brief cycle, include 3 examples of how the creator talks about products in their organic content — then mirror that language. The brief should sound like the creator wrote it before they got to the product section.

**Problem: Creator refuses to use the exact CTA language provided and substitutes a weaker version ("check it out at the link below")**
Solution: Brief the creator on *why* the specific CTA matters — "soft CTAs like 'check it out' reduce conversion by 60–70% based on our past campaigns." Give them ownership over CTA language by offering 3 pre-approved variants at different intensity levels and letting them choose. Most creators will select the medium-intensity option when given a choice rather than feeling pressured into hard-sell language.

**Problem: Creator asks to remove the sponsorship disclosure or make it less prominent**
Solution: Non-negotiable — FTC guidelines require clear and conspicuous disclosure for paid partnerships, and most platforms (LinkedIn, YouTube, Instagram) have parallel native disclosure requirements. Educate the creator on the legal risk they personally carry (disclosure violations fall on the creator, not just the brand), and frame clear disclosure as a trust-builder rather than a credibility reducer. Provide examples of top-performing sponsored posts that disclosed upfront and still achieved strong engagement — proof that audiences reward transparency.
