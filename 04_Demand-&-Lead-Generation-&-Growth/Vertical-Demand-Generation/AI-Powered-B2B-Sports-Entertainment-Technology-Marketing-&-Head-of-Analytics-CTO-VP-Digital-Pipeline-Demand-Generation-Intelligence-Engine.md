Designs a complete B2B demand generation program for technology companies selling into sports teams, leagues, media rights holders, venues, and entertainment organizations — covering the unique buyer personas (Head of Analytics, Chief Technology Officer, VP of Digital, Chief Revenue Officer of a team), the procurement cycles tied to off-seasons and broadcast rights windows, and the proof-of-ROI frameworks that win budget from owners and commissioners focused on fan revenue and competitive performance. Use this when your SaaS platform targets the sports and entertainment ecosystem and generic B2B demand gen tactics aren't converting because the buying process, proof requirements, and channel mix are fundamentally different from enterprise software.

2.1 Primary Buyer Persona: Head of Analytics / Director of Data Science

Profile:
- Title variations: Head of Analytics, Director of Strategy & Analytics, VP of Research & Analytics, Head of Performance Science
- Org size: 2-15 person analytics team at major clubs; 1-3 at smaller organizations
- Technical profile: Deep statistical background (R, Python), increasing AI/ML exposure; often came from academic sports science or professional sports data role
- Reports to: General Manager, Director of Football, Head of Performance, or Chief Operating Officer
- Budget ownership: Often influences but rarely holds final approval; presents ROI case to GM/ownership

What keeps them up at night:
- "Our models are good but we can't get buy-in from coaching staff to actually use the outputs"
- "We're collecting data from 8 different systems and spending 60% of analyst time on data wrangling rather than insight generation"
- "I need to prove the ROI of analytics to ownership, but the causal link between analytical decisions and wins/revenue is hard to quantify"
- "Our competitor just hired [top analytics person from a peer organization] — are they building something we don't have?"

Message that resonates:
- NOT: "Our platform integrates with your existing data sources." (Feature)
- YES: "[Team X] reduced player injury risk 31% in their first season and attributed 2 additional wins to analytics-informed lineup decisions using our platform — here's how their Head of Analytics made that case to their GM." (Outcome + peer proof)

Outreach channel priority: LinkedIn DM (personalized to their background), Sloan conference, email with peer reference, podcast guesting on analytics shows they listen to (Causal Inference in Sports Science, The Data Lab Sports)

2.2 Economic Buyer Persona: General Manager / Head of Football / VP of Sporting Performance

Profile:
- Title variations: General Manager, Director of Football, Head of Sporting Performance, Chief Operating Officer (Sports Ops)
- Decision authority: Controls sporting budget; typically 1-3 year contract cycles
- Technical depth: Understands data value conceptually; needs outcomes translated to wins, revenue, or competitive edge — not technical specifications
- Time horizon: Focused on season performance + long-term roster building

What they need from you:
- "Show me this works at a team I respect"
- "How does this fit within our existing analytics infrastructure without requiring a rip-and-replace?"
- "What does the contract look like — is this a pilot or a commitment?"
- "If this doesn't deliver, how do I explain it to ownership?"

Message that resonates:
- "Three of the last five NFL playoff teams renewed their contracts with us after seeing [specific outcome] in the first 6 months. Here's what the Head of Analytics at [reference team] saw — and what your team could see."

Outreach approach: Warm introduction through existing analytics relationship, or through league network (commissioner's office, league technology team). Cold outreach rarely converts. Executive roundtable invitation.

2.3 Technical/Enablement Buyer Persona: VP of Technology / Director of Data Engineering

Profile:
- Responsible for platform security, infrastructure integration, data governance

5.1 Sports Tech POC Structure (The "Pre-Season Pilot" Model)

Sports organizations are uniquely receptive to time-boxed pilots tied to their off-season or pre-season period. Structure your POC to deliver visible results before the competitive season begins — this creates urgency to close before they're consumed by the season.

8-Week POC Framework:
- Weeks 1-2: Data integration and platform setup (minimize IT burden — 1 technical resource, part-time)
- Weeks 3-4: First analysis run — deliver 3-5 specific findings to the analytics lead and one "executive summary" finding formatted for the GM presentation
- Weeks 5-6: Analyst team working session — co-build one internal model or dashboard that the analytics team owns and can present as their work (this creates internal champions)
- Weeks 7-8: Business case development with the analytics lead — translate platform outputs into a language the GM and CRO can act on

Success criteria definition (set these in writing before the POC begins):

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: Target buyers at sports organizations are impossible to reach — they don't respond to cold outreach and aren't active on LinkedIn.**
Solution: In sports tech, cold outreach rarely works. The solution is to appear in the places they are — Sloan, SportsPro, the MIT Sports Analytics community Slack, SportTechie's newsletter sponsorship, conference panels — and let them come to you. If you must do cold outreach, a peer reference ("[Analyst at Team X] mentioned I should reach out") is essentially required. Invest in building your first 3-5 reference relationships intensely before scaling outbound — the reference network is your primary demand generation channel in this vertical.

**Problem: Sports organizations are interested but deals stall — they say "let's revisit after the season."**
Solution: This is the #1 deal stall in sports tech. Three tactics: (1) Launch the POC DURING the season on a limited scope (2-3 hours per week from one analyst) so you have proof of concept before the next off-season — this keeps momentum alive; (2) Create a specific "in-season lite" package that serves as a bridge until the off-season evaluation, at a lower price point that doesn't require ownership approval; (3) Get commitment on a specific off-season kick-off date before the season starts — "If we can agree today that we'll start the full evaluation on [off-season date], I can hold that slot and allocate a dedicated implementation team to your organization."

**Problem: We have customer references but they won't go on the record publicly (common in sports tech where teams guard their competitive intelligence).**
Solution: Structure your references in tiers: (a) Public case study with named team and specific metrics — the gold standard, worth significant incentive to the customer (speaking slots at Sloan, exclusive product input sessions); (b) Anonymized category reference — "A playoff-caliber NFL team saw [metric]" — acceptable for outbound; (c) Peer-to-peer reference call under NDA — the customer talks to the prospect but the conversation is confidential — still highly effective because buyers trust peer conversations more than any marketing material. Build tiers (b) and (c) even if tier (a) isn't available yet — and prioritize converting at least one tier (a) reference in your first year as a key organizational OKR.
